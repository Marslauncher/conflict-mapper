01 // DOMAIN OVERVIEW

Cyber & Asymmetric Domain

Critical infrastructure, MITRE ATT&CK & gray zone spectrum
⚡ DOMAIN STRATEGIC ASSESSMENT

Cyberspace is now recognized as the fifth domain of warfare alongside land, sea, air, and space. Unlike geographic theaters, cyber conflict has no physical boundary — a single threat actor can simultaneously target power grids in Ukraine, financial institutions in the United States, and telecommunications infrastructure in Taiwan. The convergence of cyber operations with information warfare, drone proliferation, and AI-enabled autonomous systems creates a new asymmetric paradigm where non-state actors and smaller powers can generate strategic effects previously requiring nation-state military force.

Gray Zone Conflict Spectrum

Cyber operations occupy a deliberate gray zone between peacetime espionage and acts of war. State actors exploit this ambiguity to achieve strategic effects — degrading adversary infrastructure, stealing intellectual property, and shaping public opinion — while maintaining plausible deniability and staying below the threshold of armed conflict response. The spectrum runs from passive intelligence collection (SIGINT/HUMINT) through destructive attacks (NotPetya, Saudi Aramco), influence operations, and AI-enabled disinformation at scale.

The Tallinn Manual 2.0 — the most authoritative legal analysis of cyber conflict — has failed to produce consensus on when a cyber attack constitutes a use of force under Article 51 of the UN Charter, leaving major powers free to conduct operations that in kinetic form would trigger immediate military response. This legal vacuum is the central structural feature of the cyber domain.

02 // KEY STATE ACTORS

Cyber Power Doctrine

National doctrine & institutional architecture

🇺🇸
United States // NSA TAO + USCYBERCOM
Defend Forward / Persistent Engagement
NSA's Tailored Access Operations (TAO) and USCYBERCOM execute a "Defend Forward" doctrine — conducting offensive operations in adversary networks before threats reach US soil. USCYBERCOM's Cyber National Mission Force conducts Hunt Forward operations in partner nations (Ukraine, Lithuania, Montenegro). Known capabilities: zero-day development, supply chain operations (SolarWinds response), EW integration, and CNO at all classification levels.
🇨🇳
China // PLA SSF Network Systems Department
Integrated Network-Electronic Warfare (INEW)
PLA Strategic Support Force's Network Systems Department (formerly Technical Reconnaissance Bureaus) conducts large-scale intellectual property theft, pre-positioning in critical infrastructure, and supply chain compromise. China's doctrine emphasizes "system confrontation" — degrading adversary C4ISR before kinetic conflict. Volt Typhoon's 2023-24 pre-positioning in US critical infrastructure exemplifies this prepare-now-exploit-later strategy.
🇷🇺
Russia // GRU Unit 26165 + 74455, SVR, FSB
Information Confrontation (Informatsionnoye Protivoborstvo)
Russia's cyber operations are subordinated to broader "information confrontation" doctrine. GRU conducts destructive operations (NotPetya, Ukraine power grid). SVR focuses on intelligence collection (SolarWinds). FSB handles domestic and foreign counterintelligence cyber. Russia uniquely integrates cyber with kinetic (Ukraine war: simultaneous missile strikes + Viasat hack). Deniable through criminal proxies (REvil, LockBit with implicit tolerance).
🇮🇷
Iran // IRGC Electronic Warfare & Cyber Defense
Deterrence Through Disruption
Iran's IRGC Cyber Defense Command and Ministry of Intelligence (MOIS) conduct retaliatory and deterrent cyber operations. Iranian doctrine emphasizes asymmetric leverage — Shamoon destroyed 35,000 Aramco computers, and the Albanian government attack used a Russian playbook. Iran's capabilities grew substantially post-Stuxnet as a direct response, now employing destructive wiper malware, ransomware as cover, and supply chain attacks against Israel and Gulf states.
🇰🇵
North Korea // RGB Bureau 121 / Lazarus Group
Cyber as Hard Currency / State Survival
DPRK's Reconnaissance General Bureau controls Bureau 121 — a 6,000+ strong cyber force uniquely tasked with generating hard currency for the regime. North Korea has stolen over $3B in cryptocurrency since 2017 — financing WMD programs directly. DPRK actors combine espionage (Kimsuky) with financial crime (Lazarus SWIFT attacks, crypto exchange hacks) and destructive operations (WannaCry, Sony Pictures) in a single institutional framework.
🇮🇱
Israel // Unit 8200 / NSO Group (commercial)
Technological Superiority & Intelligence Dominance
Israel's Unit 8200 (signals intelligence) is widely considered the most technically sophisticated intelligence unit per capita in the world. Co-developed Stuxnet and Duqu/Flame with NSA. NSO Group's Pegasus spyware — used by 50+ governments — represents Israel's dual-use commercial-intelligence ecosystem. Unit 8200 veterans form the backbone of Israel's commercial cybersecurity industry (Check Point, CyberArk, SentinelOne founding teams). Operates under strict need-to-know compartmentalization with few public attributions.
🔴 ACTIVE OPERATIONS — MARCH 2026

Multiple ongoing campaigns active as of March 2026: Chinese Volt Typhoon pre-positioning in US water, energy, and transportation infrastructure (CISA Advisory 2025-AA-024) continues despite public attribution. Russian Sandworm conducted GPS jamming operations concurrent with Ukraine kinetic offensives. Iranian APT35 active against Israeli defense contractors post-October 2023. DPRK Lazarus pivot to AI company targeting and LLM model theft ongoing. Salt Typhoon telecom infiltration of US carriers (AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile) revealed December 2024 — full scope still unquantified as of March 2026.

05 // CRITICAL INFRASTRUCTURE TARGETS

Global Attack Surface

Undersea cables, power grids, DNS, GPS — the backbone of civilization
🔴 CRITICAL FINDING — INFRASTRUCTURE TARGETING

CISA's 2024 assessment identified critical infrastructure in all 16 CISA-defined sectors as actively targeted by state-sponsored threat actors. Volt Typhoon's pre-positioning in US water and power systems — combined with Salt Typhoon's penetration of US telecommunications wiretap infrastructure — represents the highest known risk to US homeland cyber security since the domain emerged. The convergence of physical infrastructure access (undersea cable patrol vessels) and cyber intrusion creates a multi-vector attack surface that defensive operations have not fully addressed.

🌊
UNDERSEA CABLES
Global Submarine Cable Network
400+ CABLES // 1.3M KM // 95% OF INTERNET TRAFFIC
The global undersea cable network carries 95% of international internet traffic and $10 trillion in daily financial transactions. Key chokepoints: GIUK Gap (North Atlantic), Red Sea narrows (Yemen conflict zone), South China Sea (Chinese maritime patrol), and the Luzon Strait. Russia's GUGI naval intelligence unit operates vessels capable of both mapping and physically severing undersea cables. Baltic cable incidents (2023-24) — likely Russian sabotage — severed Finland-Germany and Sweden-Lithuania links.

POWER GRID / ICS
National Power Grid SCADA Systems
SCADA / ICS / OT NETWORKS — LEGACY PROTOCOLS
Industrial Control Systems managing power generation, water treatment, and gas pipelines are increasingly networked but designed before modern cybersecurity. Sandworm's Industroyer2 (2022 Ukraine) demonstrated IEC 104 protocol exploitation causing substation shutdowns. CISA's 2023 Volt Typhoon advisory confirmed pre-positioned access in US energy infrastructure specifically designed for wartime disruption. Colonial Pipeline ransomware (2021, DarkSide) demonstrated cascading effects: 100M gallon/day pipeline shutdown for 6 days, fuel shortages across US East Coast.
KEY THREAT ACTORS: Russia Sandworm, China Volt Typhoon
ATTACK PROBABILITY: HIGH IN CONFLICT
🌐
DNS ROOT SERVERS
DNS Root Server Infrastructure
13 ROOT SERVER CLUSTERS // ANYCAST DISTRIBUTED
The 13 DNS root server logical addresses (distributed to 1,700+ physical instances via anycast) are the authoritative source of internet domain name resolution. Successful attack could render most of the internet non-navigable. BGP hijacking — demonstrated by China Telecom (accidental 2010, suspected deliberate 2019) — can redirect traffic through adversary infrastructure. Anycast distribution limits attack impact but concentrated operators (Verisign, ICANN, US military) present geopolitical chokepoints.
OPERATORS: Verisign, ICANN, RIPE, ARIN

Russian Information Operations Model

The Prigozhin/IRA model — Internet Research Agency, Saint Petersburg — industrialized influence operations. IRA operated at $35M/year budget, 1,000+ employees, and reached 126 million Americans before 2016 election (US Senate Intelligence Committee estimate). Core tradecraft: authentic-seeming fake personas, cross-platform amplification (Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, YouTube), divisive content amplification across racial, immigration, and political lines — not to persuade but to amplify existing divisions and suppress Democratic voter participation.

Post-Prigozhin (Wagner mutiny, 2023), operations appear to have shifted to Kremlin-controlled Structura National Technology and Social Design Center (SDC). Operation Doppelganger (EU DISINFO Lab, 2022-24) created 1,000+ fake news websites impersonating Le Monde, Der Spiegel, Spiegel Online with anti-Ukraine content — EU identified as the largest foreign information operation against EU member states in history.

China's United Front Work

China's United Front Work Department (UFWD) conducts influence operations through diaspora communities, Confucius Institutes, media influence (buying advertising in foreign newspapers, owning local media), and political financing. Operation "Spamouflage" (Meta/Google TAG, 2022-24): China's largest known influence network — pro-CCP content on 50+ platforms in 50+ languages, originally targeting Uyghur activists, expanded to US election content in 2024.

TikTok algorithm concerns: US government alleges ByteDance retained ability to promote/suppress content by key; ByteDance denied. Montana TikTok ban and federal divestiture law (April 2024, PAFACA) reflect growing concern about Chinese state influence over 170M US users. ODNI 2024 assessment: China conducted influence operations targeting House members who challenged China on Taiwan and Uyghur issues.

🎭
Deepfake Capabilities — 2025 State of Play
Consumer-grade deepfake generation (voice cloning, face-swap video) is now accessible for $0 to $100/month. In 2024: fake audio of US President Biden voter suppression robocall (New Hampshire primary); deepfake of Hong Kong CFO instructing $25M wire transfer (successful fraud); Slovak election deepfake of opposition leader allegedly accepting Russian funding. Nation-states now have access to capabilities that were classified-only 5 years ago. AI-generated synthetic media is the primary near-term information warfare escalation vector.
📲
Social Media Manipulation at Scale
Platform-level amplification algorithms reward outrage and controversy — making adversary influence operations self-sustaining once seeded. GEC (Global Engagement Center) identified Russian operations seeding content via fringe sites, which then gets amplified by authentic users outraged by the content, before reaching mainstream media. The "firehose of falsehood" doctrine: don't persuade — overwhelm. Volume > coherence. Seed 10 contradictory narratives and let the confusion itself be the weapon.
🗳️
Election Infrastructure Targeting
Russian GRU targeted election infrastructure in all 50 US states before 2016 (Senate Intel Vol. 1). Illinois voter database: 200,000 records stolen. Direct voting machine attempts in Florida. 2020: Iranian actors sent threatening emails to Florida voters posing as Proud Boys. 2024: Microsoft Threat Intelligence identified Iranian operation targeting campaign officials via phishing, successful against Roger Stone associate providing access to Trump campaign. European elections similarly targeted: France (Macron Leaks), Germany (Bundestag persistence), Spain (Catalonia referendum period).
🤖
AI-Enabled Influence Operations
OpenAI (2024) identified and terminated accounts from Russia (Doppelganger), China (Spamouflage), Iran, and Israel using GPT-4 to generate influence operation content at scale — articles, social media posts, translated content, and persona backstories. None had achieved significant organic amplification at time of disruption. However, AI lowers the cost floor dramatically: operations that required 1,000-person teams now require 10-person teams. The trajectory is toward near-zero cost influence operations indistinguishable from authentic human content.

08 // DRONE / AUTONOMOUS SYSTEMS

Asymmetric Air Domain

FPV drones, Shahed proliferation & AI-enabled autonomous targeting
⚡ STRATEGIC PARADIGM SHIFT — DRONE WARFARE

Ukraine has demonstrated that $500 FPV drones can disable $5M main battle tanks, fundamentally validating asymmetric force multiplication theory. The drone domain is the clearest cyber-asymmetric convergence point: RF jamming, GPS spoofing, AI target recognition, swarm coordination, and autonomous engagement all merge in this evolving capability. Iran's Shahed-136 design — exported to Russia, Yemen (Houthis), Lebanon (Hezbollah), and available to any IRGC proxy — represents the proliferation of precision strike capability to non-state actors at commodity prices.

🇺🇦
Ukraine // FPV Drone Revolution
First-Person View Combat Drones
Ukraine has deployed 1M+ FPV drones in 2024 alone. Commercial DJI-derived platforms modified with explosives (typically RPG warhead or 1kg shaped charge) guided by fiber-optic cable or encrypted RF. Cost: $400-800 per unit. Destroyed hundreds of T-72/T-80 tanks, BMPs, supply trucks, and Lancet-type loitering munitions. Russian electronic warfare countermeasures forced switch to fiber optic guidance (immune to jamming). Ukraine producing 2M FPV/year; Russia ~1.5M/year. Drone factories targeted by long-range strikes on both sides.
🇮🇷
Iran // Shahed Proliferation
Shahed-136 / Geran-2 Platform
Iran's Shahed-136 "kamikaze drone" — $20,000-50,000 unit, 2,500km range, GPS+INS guidance, 50kg warhead — has been exported to Russia (Geran-2 designation, 3,800+ used in Ukraine), Houthis (Yemen strikes on Saudi Aramco, UAE, Red Sea shipping), Hezbollah (Lebanon), and Iraqi PMF. The platform is reverse-engineered from commercially available components, allowing local production. Iran opened drone factory in Russia (Alabuga special economic zone). Ukraine documented 700+ Shahed variants including AI-guided seeker upgrades.
🤖
AI Targeting // Autonomous Systems
Machine Vision Target Recognition
Ukraine's "Saker Scout" AI system: machine vision identifies Russian vehicles from drone footage, classifies them by type, and recommends targeting decisions. Ukraine AI startup Palianytsia integrated machine learning into Mavic-derived reconnaissance drones. DARPA's Autonomous Multi-Domain Adaptive Swarms-of-Swarms (AMASS) program. Turkish Bayraktar TB2 uses AI-assisted target tracking. The legal and ethical threshold: when does autonomous target engagement constitute unlawful killing under IHL? International community has failed to agree on Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems (LAWS) regulations (CCW discussions stalled).
🌊
Maritime // USV Warfare
Uncrewed Surface Vehicle (USV)
Ukraine's MAGURA V5 USV — semi-autonomous sea drone — struck Russian Black Sea Fleet frigate Admiral Makarov and damaged submarine Rostov-on-Don in Sevastopol harbor. Houthis deployed USV suicide boats against Red Sea shipping. Iran tested armed USVs in Persian Gulf exercises. Cost: $250,000-500,000 vs $2B+ warship targets. ISR integration allows real-time teleoperation from 1,000km+. US Navy's Ghost Fleet Overlord program developing large autonomous surface vessels. This capability represents a direct challenge to naval power projection by major state actors.
🔌
EW / Cyber vs. Drones
Electronic Warfare Countermeasures
Russian Krasukha-4 and RB-341V Leer-3 EW systems jam commercial drone frequencies. Russian EW reportedly jamming JDAM-ER glide bombs and AIM-9X Sidewinder in Ukraine theater. GPS spoofing redirected Iranian drones during April 2024 Iran-Israel exchange. Israel used cyber operations to shut down Iranian drone swarm radar during April 2024 exchange. Ukrainian fiber-optic FPV development is specifically EW-immunity response. The drone-EW-cyber triangle is now the defining tactical technology competition in conventional conflict.
🔬
Loitering Munitions // Asymmetric
Equalizer Weapons
Loitering munitions — "suicide drones" that orbit a target area before striking — represent the ultimate asymmetric equalizer. Israeli Harop (anti-radiation), US Switchblade-300/600, Russian Lancet-3 (responsible for hundreds of destroyed Ukrainian vehicles), Turkish Kargu-2 (alleged first autonomous lethal engagement, Libya 2020 — UN Panel of Experts). DPRK's KN-24 short-range ballistic missile with loitering submunitions. Price range: $6,000 (Switchblade-300) to $500,000 (Harop). Accessible to non-state actors through black market and state patronage.
09 // PROXY FORCES & NON-STATE ACTORS

The Gray Network

Ransomware groups, hacktivists & state-affiliated criminals

Ransomware-as-a-Service Ecosystem

Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) has industrialized cybercrime. Operators (developers) build and maintain ransomware tooling; affiliates (criminals) conduct intrusions and pay 10-30% to operators. The ecosystem has created a multi-billion dollar criminal enterprise with sophisticated structures: customer support for victims negotiating payment, bug bounty programs for researchers identifying flaws, and affiliate recruitment on dark web forums. Russia's FSB has demonstrated implicit tolerance of ransomware groups operating from Russian territory as long as they avoid Russian/CIS targets — providing plausible deniability while benefiting from financial pressure on Western economies.

Notable disruptions: Operation Cronos (2024) seized LockBit infrastructure across 10 countries, arrested LockBitSupp affiliates, and unmasked LockBit administrator as Dmitry Khoroshev. Operation Endgame (2024) disrupted IcedID, SystemBC, Pikabot droppers. Despite disruptions, affiliates reconstitute quickly — RansomHub absorbed former LockBit and ALPHV affiliates within weeks. The RaaS model ensures operational resilience regardless of individual group disruption.

Hacktivist & Patriotic Hackers

Ukraine war created the largest hacktivist mobilization in history. IT Army of Ukraine (300,000+ volunteers) conducted DDoS attacks against Russian government, banking, and media targets. Anonymous (decentralized, Western) attacked Russian state TV, leaked FSB data. Pro-Russian Killnet conducted DDoS campaigns against hospitals (Netherlands), airports (US), and government sites across NATO countries. NoName057(16) (pro-Russia) runs coordinated DDoS via volunteer botnet.

Middle East conflict: Cyber Av3ngers (IRGC-affiliated) targeted Israeli water authority and US industrial control systems. SiegedSec (Western hacktivist) leaked data from Israeli and US government systems claiming anti-war motivation. Sudan Anonymous targeted hospitals in Israel and Gaza solidarity actions. The hacktivist layer provides additional gray-zone deniability and amplification to state actor campaigns while genuinely complicating attribution.

10 // LEGAL / ATTRIBUTION & RULES OF ENGAGEMENT

Ungoverned Domain

Tallinn Manual, NATO Article 5 threshold & attribution challenges

Tallinn Manual & International Law

The Tallinn Manual (2013, updated 2017 as Tallinn Manual 2.0) is the most authoritative analysis of how international law applies to cyber operations. Produced by a NATO CCDCOE-convened group of international law experts (non-binding). Key Rule 30: a cyber operation constitutes a use of force if its "scale and effects" are comparable to a kinetic use of force — but no consensus exists on where that threshold lies.

NotPetya — which caused $10B+ damage globally — was NOT treated as an act of war requiring Article 51 response despite being clearly state-sponsored destruction. The legal vacuum is intentional from Russian and Chinese perspectives: it preserves maximum operational freedom below the response threshold. NATO Cyber Defence Pledge (2016) affirms cyber attack can trigger Article 5 but provides no threshold definition.

NATO Article 5 — Cyber Threshold Debate

At NATO Vilnius Summit (2023), allies agreed cyber attacks can trigger Article 5 collective defense. But specific threshold remains deliberately vague. Key questions: Does SolarWinds-scale espionage trigger Article 5? (US answer: no). Does destructive attack on a member's power grid trigger Article 5? (Answer: depends on attribution certainty and scale). Does ICS attack causing deaths trigger Article 5? (Probably yes). The deliberate ambiguity is intended to deter while maintaining escalation control — but adversaries exploit it for maximum gray-zone operations.

Attribution Challenges

Technical attribution identifies who conducted an attack; legal/political attribution determines who is held responsible. Technical attribution has improved dramatically — five-country intelligence alliances (Five Eyes) enable corroborating signals intelligence with forensic analysis. Mandiant (Google) and CrowdStrike regularly publish high-confidence technical attribution. Political attribution remains restrained: governments declassify only what won't reveal collection methods.

Proportional Response Doctrine

US Cyber Command's "Defend Forward" doctrine authorizes operations in adversary networks before attacks reach US infrastructure — a de facto first-use posture. The 2018 US National Cyber Strategy and Presidential Policy Directive 20 revision lowered the threshold for offensive cyber operations. EU's Cyber Diplomacy Toolbox enables coordinated diplomatic responses (sanctions, expulsions) but no kinetic response option. The Geneva Convention Additional Protocols and Laws of Armed Conflict (LOAC) apply to cyber operations in armed conflict — but their application in peacetime gray-zone operations remains deeply contested.
