01 // GEOGRAPHIC OVERVIEW

Strategic Geography

Sahel corridor, Horn of Africa & maritime chokepoints
⚠ STRATEGIC OVERVIEW

The Africa theater is the world's largest contiguous landmass — 30.4 million km² hosting 54 sovereign nations — and the primary arena for 21st-century great power competition. Russia's Africa Corps (Wagner Group successor) operates across the Sahel; China controls the continent's only purpose-built foreign military base in Djibouti and anchors Belt & Road dual-use port infrastructure from the Atlantic to the Indian Ocean; the United States operates AFRICOM from Stuttgart and maintains its largest Africa base at Camp Lemonnier, Djibouti. France's withdrawal from Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger has created strategic vacuums being filled at speed by Russian and Chinese influence, making Africa the fastest-moving theater in the 2026 great power competition landscape.

Strategic Terrain Analysis

The Africa theater is defined by five strategic sub-regions that each present distinct competition dynamics. The Sahel corridor (Mali–Niger–Burkina Faso–Chad) is the active hot zone of Russian influence expansion and jihadist insurgency. The Horn of Africa — centered on Djibouti — is the world's most concentrated foreign basing hub, hosting US, Chinese, French, and Japanese forces within kilometers of each other. The Gulf of Guinea hosts Nigeria's oil infrastructure (2.1M bbl/day capacity) and growing maritime piracy operations. The Mozambique Channel is an emerging insurgency and energy flashpoint (natural gas reserves). The Cape of Good Hope corridor provides the alternative global trade route when Suez/Bab el-Mandeb is disrupted.

Africa's mineral endowment — over 30% of the world's cobalt (DRC), significant lithium, manganese, platinum, and rare earth deposits — positions the continent as a decisive theater for 21st-century technology supply chain competition. Chinese control of Zambian/Congolese cobalt through debt-for-resource infrastructure financing creates strategic leverage over global EV battery production that rivals any military asset in the theater.

02 // STRATEGIC CONTEXT

Why Africa Matters

🔴 ACTIVE COMPETITION THEATER — MARCH 2026

The Africa theater is experiencing accelerating great power competition as France's Sahel withdrawal creates strategic vacuums rapidly being filled by Russia's Africa Corps and deepening Chinese infrastructure investment. The Sahel has seen three military coups since 2020 (Mali 2021, Burkina Faso 2022, Niger 2023) — all resulting in expulsion of French forces and invitation of Russian PMC/Africa Corps operatives. China now operates the only foreign military base on the continent (Djibouti, since 2017) and controls strategic port infrastructure at Doraleh (Djibouti), Lamu (Kenya), and Bagamoyo (Tanzania). AFRICOM's counterterrorism mission is being displaced by great power competition as the primary organizing framework.

🇷🇺
Russian Strategy
Africa Corps / Wagner Successor
Russia's Africa Corps (rebranded from Wagner Group post-Prigozhin 2023) operates across 20+ African countries, providing regime security for authoritarian governments in exchange for mining concessions and strategic basing access. The model combines PMC fighters, disinformation campaigns, and resource extraction to create durable Russian spheres of influence that displace Western partners at minimal cost.
🇨🇳
Chinese Strategy
Dual-Use Infrastructure (BRI)
China's Belt & Road Initiative in Africa combines commercial port development with potential military access. The Djibouti base (PLAN Support Base, 2017) established China's first overseas military facility — with potential capacity for aircraft carriers. Chinese-built ports at Doraleh, Mombasa, Bagamoyo, and Hambantota (Indian Ocean) create a "String of Pearls" limiting US freedom of maneuver.
🇺🇸
US Strategy
AFRICOM Counterterrorism to GPC
AFRICOM (est. 2007) originally focused on counterterrorism: al-Shabaab (Somalia), ISIS-Sahel, Boko Haram. The 2022 National Defense Strategy elevated Great Power Competition (GPC) as the primary organizing mission. AFRICOM now frames Chinese basing access and Russian PMC operations as equal-priority threats to Islamist insurgency. Camp Lemonnier (4,000 troops) and Agadez drone base (Niger, suspended 2024) are keystone nodes.
🇫🇷
French Withdrawal
Barkhane Collapse & Power Vacuum
France's Operation Barkhane (2014–2022, 5,100 troops) was expelled from Mali in 2022, Burkina Faso in 2023, and Niger in 2023 after military coups. Each expulsion was followed by Africa Corps (Wagner) deployment within weeks. France retains a smaller presence in Chad and Senegal but has lost its Sahel security architecture. This represents the fastest collapse of a major power's regional security framework since Cold War decolonization.
⛏️
Resource Competition
Critical Minerals Race
Africa holds 30%+ of global cobalt (DRC), 40% of gold, significant lithium (Zimbabwe, DRC), manganese, platinum, and rare earth elements. Chinese firms control the processing and extraction of most DRC cobalt through Sicomines and CMOC joint ventures. US Executive Order 14017 identified African critical minerals as a supply chain vulnerability. Russian gold extraction in Sudan, Central African Republic, and Mali funds Africa Corps operations.
🕌
Jihadist Insurgency
IS-Sahel / JNIM Expansion
JNIM (Jama'at Nusrat al-Islam wal-Muslimin, al-Qaeda affiliate) and ISGS (Islamic State Greater Sahara) have expanded territorial control across Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger as Western CT presence withdrew. JNIM controls significant rural territory in three countries as of 2026. Africa Corps forces have failed to reverse insurgent gains despite high civilian casualties, suggesting the PMC model cannot substitute for population-centric COIN operations.

📌 KEY LESSON: THE WAGNER/AFRICA CORPS MODEL

Russia's Africa model has proven remarkably cost-effective: deploy 500–2,000 PMC fighters, provide regime security, extract gold/diamonds/minerals to fund operations, run disinformation campaigns against Western partners, and create durable Russian influence spheres at a fraction of the cost of conventional military deployments. The model works because African governments facing insurgencies and popular pressure need security quickly — and Africa Corps delivers without the human rights conditionality of Western partners. The weakness: Africa Corps has consistently failed to defeat JNIM and ISGS tactically, creating long-term insurgency dependence that perpetuates Russian involvement.

📌 KEY LESSON: DJIBOUTI AS STRATEGIC BELLWETHER

Djibouti's unique geography — controlling Bab el-Mandeb with all four major powers (US, China, France, Japan) maintaining bases within 5km of each other — makes it the most strategically sensitive real estate in Africa. A Chinese-Djiboutian renegotiation of the Doraleh Container Terminal lease (seized in 2018) and the PLA base expansion negotiations represent the clearest near-term signal of China's Africa military ambitions. Any Chinese capability to operate aircraft carriers from Djibouti would fundamentally alter the Indian Ocean balance.

06 // KEY TERRAIN & CHOKEPOINTS

Strategic Chokepoints

STRAIT
Bab el-Mandeb
29km WIDE // GATEWAY TO SUEZ / RED SEA
The "Gate of Tears" separates the Horn of Africa (Djibouti/Eritrea) from the Arabian Peninsula (Yemen). 12% of global trade and 8% of global LNG transits this passage annually en route to the Suez Canal. Djibouti — which hosts US, Chinese, French, and Japanese military bases — controls the African shore. Houthi interdiction from the Yemeni shore (2023–25) demonstrated effective non-state maritime blockade capability, reducing Red Sea transits by 65–70% without controlling the strait. The Bab el-Mandeb is also the exit point for any Chinese naval force deploying from the Djibouti base into the Indian Ocean.
CONTROLLING POWERS:Djibouti (Africa shore, multi-power bases), Yemen/Eritrea
INTERDICTION RISK:CRITICAL
CAPE ROUTE
Cape of Good Hope
SOUTHERN TIP OF AFRICA // ALTERNATE GLOBAL TRADE ROUTE
Africa's southern tip is the alternative global maritime trade route when the Suez Canal and Bab el-Mandeb are disrupted. Approximately 20,000 vessels round the Cape annually under normal conditions, rising sharply during Red Sea crises. The Houthi campaign (2023–25) diverted an estimated 2,500+ vessels via Cape route, adding 7–14 days and $1–3M per voyage. South Africa's political non-alignment (BRICS member, naval exercises with Russia and China) creates uncertainty about Cape access rights in a great power conflict involving African states.
CONTROLLING POWERS:South Africa (territorial waters; politically non-aligned)
INTERDICTION RISK:MEDIUM
STRAIT
Strait of Gibraltar (Africa Side)
14km NARROWEST POINT // MOROCCO/SPAIN
The Strait of Gibraltar's southern shore is controlled by Morocco (and the Spanish enclaves of Ceuta and Melilla). All Atlantic-Mediterranean maritime traffic — over 200 vessels per day — passes through this 14km chokepoint. Morocco's strategic position gives it significant leverage as a US/NATO partner, but ongoing Saharan sovereignty disputes and Chinese infrastructure investment in Tangier Med (Africa's largest container port) create competing alignment pressures. Any great power conflict involving North Africa would make the Gibraltar African shore a NATO priority.
CONTROLLING POWERS:Morocco (+ Spain's Ceuta/Melilla enclaves)
INTERDICTION RISK:MEDIUM
CHANNEL
Mozambique Channel
450km WIDE // INDIAN OCEAN GATEWAY
The Mozambique Channel separates Mozambique from Madagascar and carries significant Indian Ocean trade. The channel's strategic importance was dramatically elevated by the discovery of vast natural gas reserves in Cabo Delgado province (est. 150 trillion cubic feet) — among the largest finds globally. IS-Mozambique (Ansar al-Sunna) insurgency since 2017 has driven 800,000 IDPs and disrupted $20B+ in TotalEnergies, Eni, and ExxonMobil LNG projects. SADC and Rwandan military intervention (2021–present) protects energy infrastructure but has not eliminated the insurgency. Chinese port investment at Beira and Maputo creates a BRI node in the channel's approach.
CONTROLLING POWERS:Mozambique, Madagascar, South Africa (maritime patrol)
INTERDICTION RISK:ELEVATED
OIL CORRIDOR
Gulf of Guinea Oil Corridor
NIGERIA TO ANGOLA // SUB-SAHARAN ENERGY HUB
The Gulf of Guinea hosts sub-Saharan Africa's major oil-producing states — Nigeria (2.1M bbl/day capacity, reduced by theft/sabotage to ~1.3M), Angola (1.2M bbl/day), Equatorial Guinea, Gabon, and Congo-Brazzaville — collectively supplying 5–6% of global crude. China is Angola's primary export destination (60%+ of crude). Nigeria's Delta oil infrastructure faces persistent militancy and theft from the Ijaw/MEND networks. Gulf of Guinea piracy (ISWAP/Lagos-based syndicates) has declined but remains a regional security burden. AFRICOM's Gulf of Guinea maritime partnership is a key competition vector against Chinese port dominance.
CONTROLLING POWERS:Nigeria, Angola (dominant producers), ECOWAS maritime framework
INTERDICTION RISK:MEDIUM
LAND CORRIDOR
Sahel Corridor
5,400km EAST-WEST // ATLANTIC TO RED SEA
The Sahel is Africa's primary land-based strategic conflict zone — a 5,400km semi-arid belt from Senegal to Eritrea that serves as both the epicenter of Islamist insurgency expansion and the theater of Russian Africa Corps operations. Three military coups (Mali 2021, Burkina Faso 2022, Niger 2023) flipped Sahel security architectures from French/Western to Russian in under 24 months. JNIM and ISGS now control significant territory across all three coup states. The Sahel corridor is also the primary transit route for sub-Saharan migration toward Mediterranean Europe, creating EU security stakes beyond the immediate CT mission.
CONTROLLING POWERS:Junta governments (Mali/Burkina/Niger) + Russia Africa Corps; JNIM/ISGS control rural territory
INTERDICTION RISK:HIGH (STATE COLLAPSE)

Chinese Port Network Advantage
Belt & Road Strategic Depth
China's BRI port investments provide a blue-water logistics chain from the Atlantic to the Indian Ocean that no competitor can match. Chinese ports at Doraleh (Djibouti), Lamu (Kenya), Bagamoyo (Tanzania), Mombasa, Walvis Bay (Namibia), and multiple Atlantic ports create a pre-positioned logistics infrastructure that took decades and $70B+ to build. In a major contingency, PLAN could sustain naval operations from the Djibouti base while drawing on this commercial port network for resupply.
Russian Air Bridge Constraint
Ukraine War Creates Africa Logistics Competition
Russia's Il-76 and An-124 strategic airlift fleet is under severe strain from Ukraine war operations, limiting the scale of the Africa Corps air bridge. Estimated 20+ Il-76 sorties/month to Africa are drawn from the same pool supporting Ukraine operations. Equipment quality to Africa is declining — older T-72 variants and less sophisticated air defense systems than would otherwise be provided. This creates a window for Western partners to demonstrate superior equipment quality if partner nation access can be maintained.
AFRICOM Access Erosion
Niger Loss Creates ISR Gap
AFRICOM's suspension of Agadez, Niger operations (2024) has created a critical ISR and logistics coverage gap across the central Sahel. The $110M Agadez base provided MQ-9 coverage of approximately 2.5 million km² of Sahel territory from a single site. Replacement via Manda Bay (Kenya) and occasional Djibouti sorties cannot replicate central Sahel coverage geometry. French withdrawal from three Sahel states simultaneously compounds the access problem — AFRICOM is losing the forward presence infrastructure it spent 15 years building.
⚠ FRANCE WITHDRAWAL: LOGISTICS IMPACT ASSESSMENT

France's withdrawal from Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger has created a logistics vacuum that Russia has been faster to fill than any other external actor. French infrastructure at Niamey (Niger), Bamako (Mali), and Ouagadougou (Burkina Faso) included hardened airfields, fuel depots, forward ammunition storage, and medical facilities. These have transferred — with varying degrees of condition — to either junta control or Africa Corps use. The strategic consequence is that Russia now has three former French military bases that provide better infrastructure than anything Russia built from scratch in Africa. France is effectively subsidizing Russia's Africa logistics build-up through the cost of abandoned infrastructure.

09 // ESCALATION PATHWAYS

Proxy War → Great Power Confrontation

🎯 ESCALATION FRAMEWORK NOTE

The Africa theater's escalation dynamic is fundamentally different from Europe or the Pacific: the primary risk is creeping great power competition via proxies and infrastructure rather than conventional military confrontation. The escalation ladder in Africa runs from proxy competition to inadvertent confrontation between US/China forces in the compressed Djibouti basing environment — not from conventional to nuclear exchanges. The wild card is an Africa Corps action that directly kills US personnel (e.g., in a SOF advisory role in a country where Africa Corps is also operating), triggering a crisis that tests whether great powers can de-escalate in a theater with no established crisis management protocols.

10 // WILD CARDS & X-FACTORS

Black Swans & Inflection Points

🌍
African Union Military Integration
A revitalized African Standby Force (ASF) under Nigerian or South African leadership could create an indigenous security architecture that reduces dependence on all external powers simultaneously. The AU has the mandate (Article 4(h) of the AU Constitutive Act permits intervention to prevent genocide) but historically lacks the logistics, financing, and political will to sustain operations. ECOWAS's 2023 threatened intervention in Niger (ultimately not executed) shows both the potential and limitations of this model.
⚓
Chinese Naval Base Expansion (Second Base)
US intelligence assesses China is actively negotiating a second African military base, with Tanzania (Bagamoyo/Dar es Salaam), Mozambique, or Equatorial Guinea (Atlantic) as the most likely sites. An Atlantic-facing Chinese base would be qualitatively transformative — providing PLAN with operational access to the Atlantic for the first time and positioning Chinese forces adjacent to the Cape of Good Hope trade route. This is assessed as the single most consequential potential geostrategic development in Africa this decade.
⚛️
Russian Nuclear Energy Deals as Leverage
Rosatom (Russia's state nuclear energy company) is aggressively marketing nuclear power plants across Africa, with signed agreements in Egypt (El Dabaa, 4 reactors under construction), South Africa (negotiations), Nigeria, Rwanda, Ethiopia, Uganda, and Zambia. Nuclear energy deals create 60–80 year dependency relationships — Rosatom provides fuel, operations, waste management, and training. A Russian nuclear energy monopoly across Africa would give Moscow unprecedented long-term political leverage well beyond any PMC deployment.
🌊
Climate Migration Destabilization
The Sahel is experiencing 1.5x the global average rate of temperature increase. Lake Chad has shrunk 90% since 1960, displacing 2.5M people. Combined with population growth (Africa's population is projected to reach 2.5B by 2050), climate-driven resource scarcity will generate unprecedented migration and conflict pressure. Great powers that invest in African climate adaptation now will gain durable influence; those that don't will face destabilization that their military investments cannot contain. Africa Corps and BRI ports cannot address Sahel desertification.
⛏️
Critical Mineral Supply Chain Disruption
China controls ~80% of the world's cobalt refining, with DRC providing 70% of raw supply. A disruption to DRC cobalt (conflict, export ban, Chinese embargo in retaliation for Taiwan sanctions) would halt global EV production within 6 months. The US has one operational cobalt refinery. EU has none. This supply chain concentration gives China enormous economic coercion leverage that exceeds any military asset in the Africa theater — and is not susceptible to military solution. It can only be resolved by decade-long diversification investments.
🗳️
Democratic Consolidation Counter-Trend
West Africa's coup wave (2020–23) may be approaching saturation. Senegal, Ghana, and Kenya have maintained democratic governance under significant pressure. A successful African democratic model that demonstrates economic growth and security without authoritarian partners would be the most powerful counter to both Africa Corps and Chinese authoritarian-infrastructure influence. Botswana, Namibia, and Cape Verde represent stable democratic examples — if their development model scales, it undermines the Africa Corps pitch fundamentally.
🤖
Autonomous Weapons Proliferation to Non-State Actors
Turkish Bayraktar TB2 drones (used effectively by Azerbaijan and Ukraine) have proliferated to Ethiopia, Morocco, Niger, and potentially others. Chinese armed drones (Wing Loong II, Caihong series) are the most widely exported armed UAVs globally, with multiple African operators. The combination of cheap commercial drones and increasingly capable Chinese/Iranian drone technology is democratizing precision air strike capability — enabling Sahelian insurgent groups and militia forces to challenge state air defenses for the first time.
💡
Tech-Enabled African Leapfrogging
Africa has already demonstrated mobile payment leapfrogging (M-Pesa Kenya) and solar energy leapfrogging. If African states build indigenous AI/drone industries using Chinese or US investment, they may develop independent military-relevant technology that reduces dependence on all external powers. Nigeria's growing tech ecosystem, Kenya's AI innovation sector, and Rwanda's drone delivery infrastructure (Zipline) represent potential seeds of an indigenous African defense technology base that could reshape theater dynamics by 2035.
Africa Corps Model — Strengths, Weaknesses & Long-Term Trajectory

Russia's Africa Corps (Wagner successor) has achieved remarkable geopolitical returns on a relatively modest investment. For an estimated $2–3B annual cost (partially offset by resource extraction), Russia has displaced French and US influence from the Sahel's three key states, established four African military footholds (Mali, CAR, Libya, Burkina Faso), and positioned Russia as the preferred security partner for authoritarian governments facing insurgency. The model's core advantage is providing security without political conditionality — something Western partners legally cannot do.

However, Africa Corps has consistently failed to defeat JNIM and ISGS militarily. In Mali, JNIM has expanded territorial control since Africa Corps arrival. In CAR, armed groups continue operating near the capital. The pattern: Africa Corps can prevent regime collapse and punish opposition villages, but cannot win population-centric counterinsurgency. This creates a strategic dependency trap — junta governments become permanently reliant on Russian security as the insurgency endures, deepening Russia's leverage while protecting its resource extraction and basing access indefinitely.

The post-Prigozhin Africa Corps transition to Russian MoD control has reduced the organizational autonomy that made Wagner effective (flexible, fast, willing to take risks MoD won't authorize). Some African clients have noted declining operational quality since the 2023 restructuring. The Ukraine war also diverts experienced Africa Corps personnel back to Eastern Europe, creating capacity constraints that China is positioned to exploit in countries where Beijing offers infrastructure-for-security deals rather than Russia's mining-for-security model.

China's "No Conditions" Africa Model vs. Western Conditionality — The Competitive Dynamics

China's Africa investment model — "economic cooperation without political interference" — has proven highly attractive to African governments frustrated by Western human rights conditionality on aid and investment. China has built $700B+ in African infrastructure (roads, ports, railways, power plants) since 2000, primarily through BRI, with no democratic governance requirements. This creates economic relationships that outlast any government and generate durable Chinese influence regardless of who holds power.

The model's vulnerabilities are becoming apparent: debt sustainability concerns (Zambia defaulted on Chinese debt 2020; Kenya/Ethiopia face unsustainable Chinese loan burdens) have given some African governments leverage to renegotiate terms. Chinese infrastructure quality has been inconsistent — some BRI railways have near-collapsed within 5 years of completion. And China's refusal to engage with conflict mediation (unlike the US/France/AU) means Beijing cannot resolve the security crises that undermine its own infrastructure investments.

The critical competition for the US is whether it can offer a genuinely competitive alternative that addresses African governments' core needs: security + development + no lectures. The US Development Finance Corporation (DFC), Lobito Corridor railway investment ($250M), and the Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Investment (PGII) represent attempts at this — but at $500M–$1B/year in Africa, they are dwarfed by Chinese BRI commitments of $10–20B/year historically.

11 // ASSESSMENT & PROBABILITY

Theater Assessment

🔴 OVERALL ASSESSMENT — MARCH 2026

The Africa theater is at a strategic inflection point: France's Sahel architecture has collapsed, AFRICOM's access network is contracting, and Russia's Africa Corps + China's BRI are consolidating positions that will be increasingly difficult to reverse. Three variables determine theater trajectory: (1) Whether Africa Corps can extend into West Africa's anchor states (Nigeria, Côte d'Ivoire) — which would shift the entire ECOWAS security framework; (2) Whether China establishes a second military base on Africa's Atlantic coast — which would give Beijing unprecedented maritime reach from the Indian Ocean to the Atlantic; (3) Whether any African states develop sufficient internal governance capacity and economic growth to demonstrate a third model that rejects both Western conditionality and Russian/Chinese dependency. The near-term outlook (2026–28) favors continued Russia/China advance; the long-term outlook (2035+) depends on whether African demographic and economic growth generates indigenous strategic agency.
