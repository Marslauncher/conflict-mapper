# Recent Korean Peninsula published war gaming scenarios.

I now have comprehensive research across all the major published wargaming exercises. Let me write the full response.

## Published Korean Peninsula War Gaming Scenarios (2023–2026)

The Korean Peninsula has become one of the most intensively war-gamed flashpoints in contemporary strategic analysis, with a cluster of recently published scenarios from think tanks, government-linked institutions, and the intelligence community converging on a deeply alarming consensus: the peninsula will not remain a contained theater in any future conflict, nuclear escalation thresholds are lower than previously modeled, and the simultaneous-contingency problem with China/Taiwan is no longer theoretical.

***

## Atlantic Council: Guardian Tiger I \& II (2025)

The most comprehensive recently published series is the Atlantic Council's **Guardian Tiger tabletop exercises**, run by the Indo-Pacific Security Initiative with support from the Defense Threat Reduction Agency (DTRA) and published in May 2025. Both exercises set scenarios in **2030** and were played by over 60 participants including active US government officials, military officers, and non-government experts.[^1_1]

### Guardian Tiger I — North Korea Initiates

The first exercise began with North Korea launching a **limited conventional strike** on South Korean naval vessels and marine bases in the Yellow Sea and Northwest Islands, with Pyongyang claiming Seoul had violated its sovereignty. The escalation ladder ran as follows:[^1_1]

- **Phase 1:** North Korea attacks South Korean naval vessels with submarine and precision missiles; South Korea counter-strikes unilaterally.
- **Phase 2:** North Korea escalates to **chemical weapons strikes** against two South Korean military facilities; issues pre-delegation orders for tactical nuclear employment; China begins limited military intervention inside North Korean territory.
- **Phase 3 (Timeline A):** North Korea executes a **low-yield tactical nuclear demonstration**, detonating a warhead near a South Korean destroyer in the East Sea while firing nuclear-capable IRBMs over Japan. The US Blue team fractured with no consensus — proposals ranged from pulsed precision conventional strikes to nuclear strikes near Pyongyang.
- **Phase 3 (Timeline B – alternative C2 structure):** Under a new notional Northeast Asia Command (NEACOM), the US attempted an information operation targeting North Korean elites with nuclear release authority via cell-phone messaging — it had no discernible effect and North Korea quickly deactivated its network. North Korea then conducted a **low-yield nuclear ballistic missile strike against the Chinhae naval base** in South Korea. A pulsed conventional response was executed, but the US and South Korea ultimately chose negotiation over regime change.[^1_1]


### Guardian Tiger II — China Initiates Over Taiwan

The second exercise opened with a **Chinese amphibious assault on Taiwan**, which stalled due to Taiwanese resistance. North Korea immediately pivoted to a parallel coercion campaign — messaging designed to split the US-South Korea alliance and demand USFK withdrawal.[^1_1]

Key moments included:

- **China fired missiles overflying Korean airspace** to strike US bases in mainland Japan, providing only North Korea advance notice — exploiting the physical geography of the Korean Peninsula as a fire corridor.
- North Korea escalated through **SRBM demonstration launches**, underground nuclear tests, and simultaneous SOF infiltration attacks using drone-delivered munitions against USFK facilities.
- By turn 4, North Korea **conducted a low-yield nuclear strike on a South Korean airbase**, while firing unarmed nuclear-capable IRBMs bracketing Guam in a deterrence signal.[^1_1]
- The exercise director adjudicated that South Korea-US Combined Forces Command (CFC) would initiate a **counteroffensive to remove the North Korean regime** — but assessed it would likely culminate on the approach to Pyongyang due to Chinese logistical and air-defense support. Further tactical nuclear exchanges were considered probable, and Taiwan's fall was a plausible concurrent outcome.[^1_1]


### Guardian Tiger Key Findings

| Finding | Detail |
| :-- | :-- |
| **Dual-adversary deterrence failure** | If at war with China or North Korea, the US will likely be unable to deter opportunistic escalation by the other [^1_1] |
| **Different escalation signatures** | North Korea escalates *vertically and early* (nuclear threats are rapid and credible); China escalates *horizontally* across cyber, space, and geography [^1_1] |
| **War expands beyond its starting point** | A Korea conflict becomes about the global nuclear order and peninsula reunification, not just restoring the armistice [^1_1] |
| **Limited nuclear strike preparation gap** | The US lacks adequate low-yield nuclear response options; only "ineffective or excessive" nuclear responses may be available in some scenarios [^1_1] |
| **C2 architecture is inadequate** | No current unified command structure effectively handles simultaneous Korea and Taiwan theaters [^1_1] |

## NPEC: "Pyongyang Goes Nuclear in Space" (January 2023)

The Nonproliferation Policy Education Center ran a three-move simulation published in January 2023 examining **North Korea's use of a nuclear weapon in low-Earth orbit (LEO) as a precursor to conventional invasion**. The scenario is set in **2029**.[^1_2][^1_3]

### Scenario Arc

1. **Move 1 (Crisis):** A North Korean ICBM test misfires and triggers Alaska-based missile defenses; the US demands North Korea garrison its mobile missiles. North Korea places an object of unknown payload into LEO and threatens a nuclear detonation in space unless the US withdraws from the peninsula. China blocks UN sanctions.[^1_2]
2. **Move 2 (Detonation):** North Korea detonates a nominal-yield nuclear device in the northern Pacific above the atmosphere. **Most LEO commercial satellites within line of sight fail immediately** — communications, navigation, weather, internet, entertainment. Chinese, Russian, and US space station crews are irradiated. North Korean ground forces simultaneously cross the DMZ and special forces attack multiple South Korean airbases.[^1_2]
3. **Move 3 (Hot Wash):** Players assessed that the US lacked pre-planned proportionate military response options to a nuclear act in space.[^1_2]

### Key Takeaways

- Space warfare will not stay in space, and legal frameworks (Outer Space Treaty) contain critical ambiguities — detonating a nuclear device that doesn't complete a full orbit may be legally permissible under the Outer Space Treaty, according to both Pentagon and Chinese lawyers who participated.[^1_2]
- Satellite hardening and constellation reconstitution planning are inadequate — participants could not agree on *when*, *where*, or *who would win* a reconstitution race.[^1_2]
- The simulation recommended developing **non-LEO orbital alternatives and terrestrial backups** (undersea cables, high-altitude UAVs, ground-based navigation) as the only credible hedge.[^1_2]
- South Korean participants objected to US messaging that nuclear weapons would not be used, reflecting a persistent alliance divergence on extended deterrence credibility.[^1_2]

## RAND Corporation: "Three Dangerous Scenarios" (2018, Ongoing Relevance)

The RAND Corporation's foundational study, *The Korean Peninsula: Three Dangerous Scenarios*, remains the basis for most structured analytical wargaming. It identified three scenario families for the US Army that remain the primary scaffolding for subsequent modeling:[^1_4]

1. **Nuclear coercion under an expanded arsenal:** North Korea uses nuclear threats and demonstrations to deter US/ROK response to limited conventional provocations — not to win a war, but to extract concessions while the nuclear umbrella shields sub-threshold attacks.[^1_4]
2. **Limited war with Seoul as the prize:** Artillery massed in the **Kaesong Heights** can strike Seoul with minimal warning. Two sub-scenarios were wargamed — a counterfire air/artillery campaign against North Korean long-range guns, and a limited ground incursion to seize the Heights. Both showed high escalatory risk and civilian casualty sensitivity.[^1_4]
3. **Regime collapse / "Nukes on the Loose":** Posited Kim Jong-un's sudden death triggering a three-faction civil war in 2017. A ship carrying special nuclear material traced to North Korea is interdicted in the Philippines, forcing international intervention. The scenario concluded that **the international community would remain on the sidelines until nuclear material moved off-peninsula**, creating an acute window of vulnerability.[^1_4]

The Heritage Foundation's **2026 Index of US Military Strength** directly references RAND analysis, noting that by 2027 North Korea could have **200 nuclear weapons and several dozen ICBMs** — a force scale that would fundamentally change the calculus of any of these scenarios.[^1_5][^1_6]

## DNI/NIC Declassified NIE: Three Scenarios Through 2030

The Office of the Director of National Intelligence published a declassified National Intelligence Estimate titled *North Korea: Scenarios for Leveraging Nuclear Weapons Through 2030*, which outlined three strategic pathways for how Kim Jong-un could employ a growing arsenal:[^1_7]


| Scenario | Description | Assessed Likelihood |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Coercion Strategy** | Continue using nuclear capabilities to intimidate neighbors, extract concessions, and bolster domestic legitimacy through sub-threshold attacks | **Most likely** |
| **Offensive / Revisionist Strategy** | Use force including nuclear weapons to shift the balance of power, dominate the South, or achieve reunification under Pyongyang | **Much less likely** (requires Kim to believe he can overmatch ROK while deterring US and retaining China's support) |
| **Defensive / Deterrence-Only** | Forgo coercive use; rely purely on existential deterrence | **Very unlikely** |

The NIE assessed that North Korea is unlikely to use nuclear weapons unless Kim believes his **regime's survival is directly threatened**, but that he is likely to take **increasingly escalatory conventional risks** believing his nuclear arsenal will deter a disproportionate response.[^1_7]

## Modern War Institute (West Point): Drone Warfare and Korean Armor (March 2026)

West Point's Modern War Institute published a scenario-based analysis in March 2026 directly linking Ukraine lessons to the Korean theater. The core finding drew on the **Atlantic Council's Guardian Tiger I and II exercises**: wargaming confirmed that a **dual contingency** — simultaneous Taiwan conflict and Korean Peninsula escalation — would create compounding vulnerability. US ISR assets and precision-guided munitions, finite in supply, would be drained by the Taiwan theater, leaving USFK exposed.[^1_8]

The MWI piece specifically highlighted the **Hedgehog 2025 NATO exercise in Estonia**, where a Ukrainian team of ~10 operators using frontline drone tactics simulated destruction of the equivalent of two full armored battalions in a single day — and extrapolated directly that any US/ROK armored force operating on the Korean Peninsula would face analogous attrition from North Korean drone swarms combined with constant ISR coverage from the opening hours of conflict.[^1_8]

## 38 North / APLN: Nuclear-Cognitive Warfare (April 2025)

Published by 38 North, this analysis introduced the concept of **nuclear-cognitive warfare** as a distinct scenario category — one in which North Korea does not need to use nuclear weapons to achieve strategic effects. The scenario framework involves four escalating stages:[^1_9]

1. **Existential coercion** — possession alone deters conventional military responses.
2. **Nuclear narratives** — sophisticated messaging campaigns using cyber and information operations to manipulate threat perceptions in Seoul and Washington.
3. **Multi-domain integration** — nuclear threats become embedded in hybrid warfare operations, linking kinetic, cyber, and information domains.
4. **Cognitive domain operations** — military operations designed to fracture allied decision-making, with nuclear weapons as the enabling threat rather than the primary weapon.[^1_9]

The scenario analysis found that North Korea's greatest vulnerability to this approach is the **differential threat perception between Seoul and Washington**: Seoul fears tactical nuclear strikes existentially; Washington prioritizes ICBMs targeting the homeland. Pyongyang exploits this divergence to undermine alliance cohesion without firing a single weapon.[^1_9]

## Freedom Shield 26 (March 2026) — Live Exercise Scenarios

In March 2026, the US and South Korea conducted **Freedom Shield 26**, an 11-day primarily computer-simulated exercise designed around "realistic wartime scenarios reflective of contemporary combat trends". The exercise ran March 9–19 and involved approximately 18,000 South Korean troops and an undisclosed number of US forces from USFK. North Korea responded during the exercise window by **launching approximately 10 ballistic missiles into the East Sea**, conducting what Pyongyang described as a firepower drill with 600mm ultra-precision multiple rocket launchers personally attended by Kim Jong-un. The exercise's war scenarios incorporated lessons from the Ukraine conflict and modeled operations across land, sea, air, space, and cyber domains simultaneously.[^1_10][^1_11]

## Dual-Contingency Scenario: Korea + Taiwan (2025)

A separate body of analysis, anchored by a Global Taiwan Institute paper from August 2025, explicitly wargames what a **simultaneous Korea-Taiwan contingency** would look like and what South Korea's options would be. The analysis — aligned with Guardian Tiger II results — found three realistic South Korean contributions:[^1_12]

1. **Securing Sea Lines of Communication (SLOCs):** Keeping the Korean theater stable so North Korea cannot spill aggression toward Japan.[^1_12]
2. **Enabling allied mobility:** Allowing USFK assets to be strategically repositioned without triggering a Korean land war.[^1_12]
3. **Preserving deterrence on the DMZ:** Preemptive deployment of missile defense, counter-battery capability, and rapid-reaction units to lock North Korea in place.[^1_12]

The paper concluded that **US-Japan-ROK trilateral tabletop exercises tailored to a dual contingency must be institutionalized immediately** — they currently exist only in ad hoc form.[^1_12]

## Current Threat Context (ISW, June 2026)

The Institute for the Study of War's most recent Korean Peninsula update (June 2, 2026) noted that North Korea is actively testing a **modular missile launcher resembling the US HIMARS system** — a hybrid platform that can carry both conventional warheads and, potentially, nuclear warheads. Kim Jong-un has directed the KPA to **disperse nuclear launch capability across a wider variety of conventional-appearing platforms**, directly complicating US and ROK targeting in any pre-emptive strike scenario. Separately, Kim summoned all KPA brigade and division commanders in May 2026 to order strengthened frontline fortifications and structural reorganization aligned with battlefield lessons from Ukraine.[^1_13][^1_14]

The OPCON transfer — shifting wartime operational control from the US to South Korea — is now on an accelerated timeline targeting completion by Q2 FY2029, before the end of Trump's second term, with Seoul explicitly seeking to guarantee the transition before potential US policy reversal.[^1_15][^1_13]

## Cross-Scenario Convergence

Across all published exercises and scenarios, several conclusions appear consistently:

- **Early nuclear use is the baseline North Korean playbook**, not the extreme case. Every Guardian Tiger game, the NIE, and 38 North's cognitive warfare analysis converge on Pyongyang deploying nuclear threats and demonstrations before any conventional fight reaches a decisive stage.[^1_7][^1_9][^1_1]
- **The US has inadequate response options in the limited nuclear use space** — the gap between conventional responses (possibly insufficient to deter) and full nuclear retaliation (politically unsustainable) remains dangerously open.[^1_1]
- **China's role is pivotal and undermodeled.** In every scenario where North Korea faces existential pressure, Chinese intervention — at minimum logistically and through air-defense support — is adjudicated as likely, dramatically reducing the viability of any regime-change operation.[^1_1]
- **The Korean theater is not separable from the Taiwan theater**. Munitions, ISR assets, command attention, and allied coordination bandwidth are all shared resources. A war started over Taiwan places USFK in an operationally degraded posture within weeks.[^1_8][^1_12]
- **Space and cyber domain attacks may precede or accompany the first kinetic shots.** The NPEC space wargame and Freedom Shield 26 both modeled these as opening-phase actions, not escalatory responses.[^1_10][^1_2]
