03Scenario Deep Review

CSIS invasion game // 24 iterations

Full Amphibious Invasion

The CSIS game is the invasion baseline: China attempts to seize Taiwan quickly through missile suppression, naval/air isolation, amphibious landings, and airborne/special operations. The game repeatedly produces heavy losses for all sides, but the decisive variable is not the first salvo. The decisive variable is whether Taiwan remains coherent long enough for US and Japanese-access-enabled combat power to attrit the follow-on lift.

  • Played friction: PLA sealift losses, beachhead sustainment, runway repair, submarine attrition of escorts/transports, and US base access.
  • Cross-game result: Surprise improves the PLA opening position, but it does not solve the logistics problem of feeding a lodgment under air, submarine, mine, and missile attack.
  • Watch-page translation: ferry requisition, RO/RO staging, hospital/blood movement, and port restrictions should outweigh routine aircraft-count spikes.
Core variableFollow-on lift survival under submarine, mine, air, and missile attrition
Decision stressWhether Taiwan remains coherent until US/Japan-enabled combat power arrives
Best watch markersRO/RO requisition, ferry conversion, port loading, medical logistics, runway repair posture

Outcome hinges less on first-salvo damage than on whether PLA follow-on lift survives. Taiwan coherence, runway repair, submarine attrition, and Japanese base access decide whether a lodgment becomes sustainable. Logistics mobilization and port controls are stronger indicators than routine air incursions.

CSIS / MIT blockade game

Blockade Or Quarantine

Lights Out shifts the problem from rapid seizure to coercive strangulation. The game stresses shipping insurance, legal framing, commercial behavior, energy and food stockpiles, cable/port resilience, and allied tolerance for convoy risk. It is slower than invasion but can be politically harder because Beijing can blur war, law enforcement, customs inspection, and economic coercion.

  • Played friction: whether commercial shipping self-deters, whether allies escort cargoes, and whether Taiwan can sustain public confidence during supply shock.
  • Cross-game result: blockade outcomes depend on coalition legal unity and economic endurance more than on a single naval engagement.
  • Watch-page translation: exclusion boxes, insurer advisories, customs notices, undersea cable incidents, and coast guard/militia density are leading indicators.

CNAS strategic-operational game

Escalation And Preemption

Dangerous Straits is useful because it refuses the clean one-theater premise. It foregrounds preemption pressure, escalation management, nuclear signaling, and the political difficulty of winning without expanding the war. The operational map is therefore not only Taiwan. It includes Okinawa, Guam, Japanese authorization, US homeland risk, cyber effects, and the credibility of off-ramps.

  • Played friction: early strikes on bases, alliance consultation speed, and escalation control after either side suffers major losses.
  • Cross-game result: even a tactically successful defense can become strategically unstable if escalation pathways are unmanaged.
  • Watch-page translation: Japanese political signals, Guam hardening, bomber/tanker movement, and PRC nuclear messaging belong on the Taiwan board.

Red-team coercive sequence

Taking Taiwan From Beijing's Perspective

The War on the Rocks red-team article is valuable because it models political objectives and coercive sequencing from the PRC side rather than starting with a US defensive order of battle. It frames force as one part of a larger campaign: information pressure, military intimidation, diplomatic isolation, legal warfare, and calibrated escalation designed to make resistance appear futile.

  • Played friction: whether coercion can create enough panic and political paralysis before outside support becomes credible.
  • Cross-game result: Beijing prefers pathways that preserve off-ramps and minimize regime-threatening military failure.
  • Watch-page translation: propaganda synchronization, diplomatic threats, lawfare language, and selective mobilization matter alongside kinetic indicators.

Justice Mission / Han Kuang

Exercise-To-Crisis Conversion

Justice Mission and Han Kuang references are not classic tabletop games, but they are essential scenario material because real drills define the warning problem. The hardest intelligence question is whether an exercise is posturing, rehearsal, or cover. The page should therefore evaluate repeat exercise geometry, logistics tails, civil-military mobilization, live-fire areas, and whether Taiwan's reserve/civil-defense system is moving from demonstration to wartime function.

  • Played friction: deciding when to mobilize without accelerating the crisis or exhausting the force during repeated false alarms.
  • Cross-game result: exercises are meaningful when they change logistics, political control, or civilian behavior, not merely when aircraft cross a line.
  • Watch-page translation: emergency legislation, port closure orders, reserve activation, distributed C2, and cyber/media restrictions are higher signal.

Korea / Japan / Indo-Pacific coupling

Dual-Contingency Stress

The dual-contingency literature links Taiwan to Korea because the same assets are consumed in both theaters: ISR, precision munitions, missile defense, tankers, submarines, political attention, and Japanese base access. The combined result is not simply additive. A Korea crisis can alter the Taiwan outcome by changing available missile-defense inventory and US/Japan political risk tolerance.

  • Played friction: simultaneous missile salvos, DPRK opportunism, PRC horizontal escalation, and competition for scarce strike and air-defense assets.
  • Cross-game result: Japan and Guam become shared operating nodes; their vulnerability couples the theaters even when shooting starts in only one.
  • Watch-page translation: DPRK launch tempo and Japan base politics should be visible on Taiwan Strait assessments.

04Operational Geometry

What The Map Is Testing

Taiwan's geometry is not a generic island diagram. Invasion games should focus on cross-strait lift, west-coast lodgment sustainment, Penghu/Kinmen/Matsu forward geography, runway repair, port denial, and anti-ship fires. Blockade games shift outward to the Taiwan Strait, Luzon/Bashi access, East China Sea/Okinawa approaches, and commercial energy/shipping routes.

  • Western coastal plain: the decisive urban, port, airfield, and logistics belt.
  • Central Mountain Range: a maneuver barrier that complicates rapid interior expansion.
  • Bashi/Luzon and Okinawa approaches: routes that determine blockade endurance and allied access.

Current Relevance

CSIS invasion and blockade games converge on the same terrain logic: ports and airfields must be denied, repaired, or opened; lift must be sustained across contested water; and political endurance depends on whether Taiwan can keep essential supply, communications, and command functions alive. The map layer should therefore support indicator tracking, not decorative arrows.

05Scenario Families

Amphibious Invasion

CSIS and related invasion games emphasize the opening campaign: missile strikes against air bases and C2, airborne and special operations attempts, cross-strait sealift, beachhead survival, and US/Japan access. The scenario is decisive but exposes the PLA to attrition if Taiwan preserves distributed fires and allied submarines/airpower enter early.

Maritime Blockade / Quarantine

Blockade games test whether Beijing can coerce surrender without the risks of immediate landing. The central contest becomes legal framing, insurance and shipping behavior, stockpile endurance, escalation thresholds, and whether the United States can reopen sea lanes without firing the first shot.

Exercise-to-Crisis

Justice Mission and Han Kuang references highlight the gray area between exercises and attack preparation. Mobilization indicators, exclusion zones, air-defense posture, reserve call-ups, and logistics flow become more important than public rhetoric.

Dual Contingency

Several sources connect Taiwan to Korea, Japan, and the wider Indo-Pacific. The key stressor is not only force count but command attention, munitions depth, ISR allocation, base vulnerability, and allied political coordination under simultaneous pressure.

08Escalation Model

Phase 0
Political shaping and ambiguity. Cyber activity, influence operations, coast guard pressure, legal warfare, and exercises create uncertainty over intent.
Phase 1
Isolation campaign. Missile threats, air and maritime exclusion zones, port interdiction, undersea cable risk, and economic pressure attempt to separate Taiwan from external support.
Phase 2
Joint fires and access denial. PLA Rocket Force, air, naval, cyber, and space effects target airfields, ports, C2, and US/Japanese staging nodes.
Phase 3
Decision point. Beijing chooses between blockade continuation, limited seizure, full landing, pause, or negotiated off-ramp based on losses, allied response, and domestic political risk.
Phase 4
Endurance contest. Munitions, fuel, repair capacity, civilian resilience, and alliance cohesion determine whether the opening shock becomes coercive success or strategic failure.

09Cross-Game Findings

Surprise Is Necessary But Insufficient

Invasion outcomes depend less on the first missile salvo than on whether follow-on sealift, air cover, and beachhead logistics survive long enough to become operationally meaningful.

Japan Is a Theater, Not a Rear Area

US intervention almost immediately makes Japanese bases, airspace, and political authorization central to campaign feasibility and escalation management.

Blockade Creates Legal and Political Ambiguity

A quarantine can force Taiwan and allies into a slower choice between concession, risky convoy operations, or accepting severe economic degradation.

Failure Is Regime-Level Risk for Beijing

Several referenced analyses stress that a failed invasion would be catastrophic for Xi Jinping politically, not merely a military setback.

10Assumptions And Limits

Common Game Assumptions

Most published games simplify mobilization warning, political decision speed, munitions replenishment, cyber effects, civilian resilience, and intra-alliance disagreement. Treat outcomes as structured stress tests, not forecasts. The most useful output is the set of recurring constraints that appear across games with different sponsors and rule sets.

Where Games Diverge

Outcomes shift sharply based on whether Japan grants access early, whether the United States prepositions munitions, whether Taiwan disperses forces before the first salvo, whether PRC leaders accept strikes on US bases, and whether blockade rules treat commercial shipping, energy cargo, and cyber disruption as military targets.

Confidence

High confidence: direct invasion is costly and politically risky for Beijing. Medium confidence: blockade/quarantine is more likely than immediate full invasion as a coercive option. Lower confidence: exact timing, allied intervention speed, and PRC tolerance for escalation against US/Japanese bases.

Analyst Use

Use this page as a bridge between source material and the live China/Taiwan Watch page. Live reporting should be mapped to game-derived indicators: logistics, exercises, exclusion zones, cyber activity, allied posture, and political signaling.

12Planning Implications

Indicators To Monitor

RO/RO shipping, civilian ferry requisition, blood supply movement, missile unit dispersal, amphibious staging, PLA reserve mobilization, information control, and unusual port restrictions should carry more weight than routine aircraft counts alone.

Defensive Priorities

Distributed anti-ship fires, runway repair, hardened C2, mobile air defense, mine warfare, resilient satellite/terrestrial communications, and civilian stockpile planning remain the highest-yield mitigation categories across scenarios.
