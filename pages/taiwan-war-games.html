Bottom line: The published Taiwan war-game literature converges on a hard operational truth: a direct invasion is costly and fragile even when China gains surprise, while blockade or quarantine scenarios can create slower but severe political, economic, and escalation dilemmas. Taiwan survivability, US/Japan access, logistics, missile inventory, airbase repair, and political endurance are the recurring decision variables.

03Scenario Families

Amphibious Invasion

CSIS and related invasion games emphasize the opening campaign: missile strikes against air bases and C2, airborne and special operations attempts, cross-strait sealift, beachhead survival, and US/Japan access. The scenario is decisive but exposes the PLA to attrition if Taiwan preserves distributed fires and allied submarines/airpower enter early.

Maritime Blockade / Quarantine

Blockade games test whether Beijing can coerce surrender without the risks of immediate landing. The central contest becomes legal framing, insurance and shipping behavior, stockpile endurance, escalation thresholds, and whether the United States can reopen sea lanes without firing the first shot.

Exercise-to-Crisis

Justice Mission and Han Kuang references highlight the gray area between exercises and attack preparation. Mobilization indicators, exclusion zones, air-defense posture, reserve call-ups, and logistics flow become more important than public rhetoric.

Dual Contingency

Several sources connect Taiwan to Korea, Japan, and the wider Indo-Pacific. The key stressor is not only force count but command attention, munitions depth, ISR allocation, base vulnerability, and allied political coordination under simultaneous pressure.

05Escalation Model

Phase 0
Political shaping and ambiguity. Cyber activity, influence operations, coast guard pressure, legal warfare, and exercises create uncertainty over intent.
Phase 1
Isolation campaign. Missile threats, air and maritime exclusion zones, port interdiction, undersea cable risk, and economic pressure attempt to separate Taiwan from external support.
Phase 2
Joint fires and access denial. PLA Rocket Force, air, naval, cyber, and space effects target airfields, ports, C2, and US/Japanese staging nodes.
Phase 3
Decision point. Beijing chooses between blockade continuation, limited seizure, full landing, pause, or negotiated off-ramp based on losses, allied response, and domestic political risk.
Phase 4
Endurance contest. Munitions, fuel, repair capacity, civilian resilience, and alliance cohesion determine whether the opening shock becomes coercive success or strategic failure.

06Cross-Game Findings

Surprise Is Necessary But Insufficient

Invasion outcomes depend less on the first missile salvo than on whether follow-on sealift, air cover, and beachhead logistics survive long enough to become operationally meaningful.

Japan Is a Theater, Not a Rear Area

US intervention almost immediately makes Japanese bases, airspace, and political authorization central to campaign feasibility and escalation management.

Blockade Creates Legal and Political Ambiguity

A quarantine can force Taiwan and allies into a slower choice between concession, risky convoy operations, or accepting severe economic degradation.

Failure Is Regime-Level Risk for Beijing

Several referenced analyses stress that a failed invasion would be catastrophic for Xi Jinping politically, not merely a military setback.

07Assumptions And Limits

Common Game Assumptions

Most published games simplify mobilization warning, political decision speed, munitions replenishment, cyber effects, civilian resilience, and intra-alliance disagreement. Treat outcomes as structured stress tests, not forecasts. The most useful output is the set of recurring constraints that appear across games with different sponsors and rule sets.

Where Games Diverge

Outcomes shift sharply based on whether Japan grants access early, whether the United States prepositions munitions, whether Taiwan disperses forces before the first salvo, whether PRC leaders accept strikes on US bases, and whether blockade rules treat commercial shipping, energy cargo, and cyber disruption as military targets.

Confidence

High confidence: direct invasion is costly and politically risky for Beijing. Medium confidence: blockade/quarantine is more likely than immediate full invasion as a coercive option. Lower confidence: exact timing, allied intervention speed, and PRC tolerance for escalation against US/Japanese bases.

09Planning Implications

Indicators To Monitor

RO/RO shipping, civilian ferry requisition, blood supply movement, missile unit dispersal, amphibious staging, PLA reserve mobilization, information control, and unusual port restrictions should carry more weight than routine aircraft counts alone.

Defensive Priorities

Distributed anti-ship fires, runway repair, hardened C2, mobile air defense, mine warfare, resilient satellite/terrestrial communications, and civilian stockpile planning remain the highest-yield mitigation categories across scenarios.
