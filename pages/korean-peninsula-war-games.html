Common Planning Horizon
Bottom line: The recent Korean war-game literature is more nuclear-forward than most Taiwan modeling. North Korea is consistently modeled as escalating early, using nuclear threats or demonstrations to fracture allied decision-making before a conventional campaign can reach a decisive stage.

02Scenario Families

Guardian Tiger I — North Korea Initiates

North Korea begins with limited conventional strikes, then escalates toward chemical use, tactical nuclear pre-delegation, nuclear demonstration, and potential nuclear strike against South Korean military infrastructure. The central lesson is allied indecision under limited nuclear use.

Guardian Tiger II — China Initiates Over Taiwan

A Taiwan war triggers opportunistic North Korean coercion. Missile overflights, SOF/drone attacks, nuclear demonstrations, and Chinese support create a two-theater stress test where Korea and Taiwan compete for munitions, ISR, command attention, and political bandwidth.

Pyongyang Goes Nuclear in Space

NPEC's scenario tests a nuclear detonation in low-Earth orbit as a precursor to conventional invasion. The game exposes major gaps in satellite resilience, legal framing, allied response options, and proportionate deterrence after nuclear use outside the atmosphere.

DNI/NIC Nuclear Leverage Pathways

The declassified NIE outlines coercive, offensive/revisionist, and defensive-only nuclear strategies. The most likely path is not an immediate all-out attack; it is increasing conventional risk-taking under a nuclear shield.

RAND Dangerous Scenarios

RAND's framework remains foundational: nuclear coercion, limited war with Seoul at risk, and regime collapse with loose nuclear material. These are not separate problems; a crisis can migrate from one family to another quickly.

Nuclear-Cognitive Warfare

38 North/APLN analysis shows how Pyongyang can use nuclear narratives, cyber, information operations, and differing threat perceptions in Seoul and Washington to fracture alliance choices without firing a nuclear weapon.

03Escalation Ladder

Move 1
Provocation under nuclear cover. Limited strikes, missile tests, cyber events, or maritime incidents are framed as defensive responses while Pyongyang threatens disproportionate escalation.
Move 2
Alliance split attempt. DPRK messaging targets Seoul's fear of tactical nuclear strikes and Washington's homeland-risk calculation, attempting to separate ROK and US decision timelines.
Move 3
Demonstration or limited nuclear use. Games repeatedly test low-yield demonstration, strike near naval forces, strike against air bases, or nuclear effects in space.
Move 4
Counteroffensive dilemma. A regime-change operation may be militarily attractive after nuclear use, but China support, DPRK residual nuclear capability, and Seoul exposure make escalation control fragile.
Move 5
Negotiation versus termination. Most games expose inadequate options between limited conventional response and excessive nuclear retaliation; termination often becomes political before it is operationally clean.

04Cross-Scenario Findings

Early Nuclear Signaling Is Baseline

North Korea's nuclear arsenal is modeled as an enabling shield for coercion, not merely an end-stage last resort.

Limited Nuclear Response Gap

Published games repeatedly find US/ROK options too weak at the conventional end and too escalatory at the nuclear end.

China Is the Swing Variable

Chinese logistics, border control, air defense support, or parallel Taiwan operations can reshape every Korean scenario.

Korea and Taiwan Are Coupled

ISR, munitions, basing, command attention, and Japanese political authorization link the theaters even when conflict begins in only one.

Space and Cyber Are Opening Domains

NPEC and Freedom Shield-style scenarios treat space/cyber effects as initial conditions, not late escalation.

Alliance Perception Gaps Matter

Seoul, Washington, and Tokyo do not experience risk symmetrically; Pyongyang can exploit those seams through information operations and nuclear threats.

05Planning Implications

Operational Priorities

  • Harden air bases, C2, missile defense, logistics, and runway repair.
  • Pre-plan limited nuclear-use response options below full retaliation.
  • Build drone, counter-drone, counter-artillery, and ISR resiliency for the first 72 hours.
  • Exercise dual-contingency command structures with Japan and Taiwan scenarios.

Strategic Warning Indicators

  • Unusual KPA brigade/division commander summons or frontline fortification orders.
  • Launcher dispersal across conventional-looking platforms.
  • SOF, drone, cyber, or satellite activity synchronized with exercises.
  • Chinese border logistics or air-defense posture changes during a Korea crisis.
