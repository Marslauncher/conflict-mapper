// EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Heritage Foundation's TIDALWAVE project — an AI-enabled simulation of a protracted U.S.–China conflict over Taiwan — constructed its fuel and logistics models against a set of baseline assumptions about global oil market conditions, China's strategic petroleum reserves, and chokepoint vulnerability. Since publication, the geopolitical landscape has shifted dramatically. On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel struck Iran, triggering the closure of the Strait of Hormuz and what the International Energy Agency has characterized as the "largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." This analysis reviews each of the nine TIDALWAVE appendices (A through I), then examines how the unfolding Iran conflict and Hormuz crisis materially alters, validates, or overturns the report's key assumptions and judgments.[1][2]

The short answer: the Iran crisis both vindicates TIDALWAVE's core thesis — that fuel logistics are a decisive wartime constraint — and simultaneously undermines several of its quantitative baselines, particularly regarding China's strategic petroleum reserve size, the sequencing of supply disruptions, and the bifurcation of U.S. military attention between the Indo-Pacific and the Middle East.

APP-A
China's Redirection of Civilian Fuel
Heritage judges with moderate confidence that China could redirect 25–30% of its civilian fuel consumption — approximately 4.0 to 4.8 million barrels per day (bpd) — to military use under full wartime mobilization, with that redirection sustainable for 60 to 100 days before inducing "cumulative economic degradation, civil transportation paralysis, and rising social instability."
China's total national demand baseline is approximately 16 million bpd, of which ~12.8 million bpd is civilian. The legal architecture enabling this is the National Defense Mobilization Law of 2010 and its 2020 amendments, which authorize the requisitioning of civilian fuel from state-owned enterprises like Sinopec and CNPC. The Military-Civil Fusion doctrine provides the coordination mechanism between those SOEs and the PLA.[3]

Heritage estimated China's Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR) at approximately 684 million barrels of crude, with total storage capacity (including commercial stocks) potentially reaching 1.8 billion barrels. Each 300 million barrel increment was modeled to provide approximately 100 days of buffer coverage at a 3 million bpd net deficit rate. The alternative, pessimistic judgment was that redirection capability may be only 10–15% — yielding PLA fuel shortfalls within 30–45 days — if enforcement fails, reserves are smaller than reported, or civilian unrest constrains mobilization.[3]

APP-B
F-76 Naval Distillate Throughput for U.S. Forces
Heritage assessed that the United States could sustain a maximum wartime F-76 delivery throughput of approximately 265,000–280,000 bpd to operational end-users in a Taiwan conflict, under idealized conditions with no interdiction or battle damage.
This estimate reflects three delivery routes: the primary U.S. sealift and Defense Fuel Supply Point (DFSP) network capped at ~200,000 bpd; bilateral access nodes at Subic Bay, Cam Ranh Bay, Port Blair, and Sabang adding 65,000–80,000 bpd; and prepositioned afloat assets excluded from the additive total. The modeled F-76 demand ceiling is approximately 370,000 bpd, creating a structural shortfall of ~90,000–105,000 bpd even under optimal conditions. The critical bottleneck is not upstream refinery or sealift capacity — both are sufficient — but at-sea replenishment capability, particularly the throughput limits of U.S. fleet oilers.[4]

APP-C
Integrated Deployment Ratio and Timing
Heritage assessed that approximately two-thirds of U.S. aircraft and surface naval combatants could be available for deployment within the first 15 days of a Taiwan conflict.
Forward-based forces in Japan and Guam are assessed as combat-ready within Days 0–5; Hawaii and West Coast surge forces arrive Days 6–15; CONUS-based tactical aircraft and ships reach theater between Days 13–30. Approximately 15–20% of aircraft and ships are assessed as non-deployable throughout a 365-day conflict due to depot-level maintenance cycles, nuclear refueling, and component shortfalls. This framework is premised on the United States not having a significant concurrent military operation consuming logistics lift and forward basing in another theater.[5]

D
APP-D
Logistics and Survivability of U.S. Navy Replenishment Tankers
Heritage assessed that U.S. F-76 fuel delivery is critically dependent on the survivability of underway replenishment assets, particularly Kaiser-class T-AO oilers.
The Military Sealift Command operates 15 T-AOs, with 7–8 typically forward deployed, and can surge 12–15 replenishment vessels within 30–60 days based on historical precedents from Desert Storm and RIMPAC exercises. However, the loss of even 2–3 T-AOs would likely reduce daily throughput by 50,000–80,000 bpd, pushing sustained fuel delivery below 200,000 bpd. PLA doctrine (derived from Science of Campaigns) explicitly prioritizes strikes on logistics vessels, and PLA strike systems — including the DF-26 anti-ship ballistic missile, YJ-18 anti-ship cruise missiles, and PLAN submarines — are capable of targeting fleet oilers within the first island chain. Heritage models throughput degradation to 185,000–215,000 bpd with a 2–3 T-AO loss in the first 7–21 days of conflict.[6]

APP-E
PLAAF Top-Tier Bases for Taiwan Contingency
Heritage identified 21 PLAAF airfields as the top-tier basing network for a Taiwan contingency, based on hardened runway infrastructure, presence of J-20, J-16, Su-35, and H-6K/U units, and geographic proximity to Taiwan.
The forward ring in Fujian and eastern Guangdong — including Longtian (~170 km from Taipei), Huian (~220 km), Shuimen (~250 km), and Zhangzhou (~400 km) — constitutes the hardened strike complex opposite Taiwan, while depth bases at Wuhu, Changxing, and Nanchang Xiangtang host J-20 and J-16 stealth reserves. Southern Theater assets at Suixi (Su-35), Foshan (J-20A), and Shaodong/Leiyang (H-6K bombers) extend southern-axis reach into the Philippine Sea and around the Bashi Channel. Northern Theater bases at Jining, Weifang, and Anshan host J-20 reserves that influence how many U.S./Japanese assets can concentrate on Taiwan versus Northeast Asia.[7]

APP-F
PLA Fuel Reserve Inventory and Drawdown Logic
Heritage assessed that PLA combined strategic and operational fuel reserves are likely sufficient to sustain major combat operations for multiple months.
Theater-level "war readiness oil" at air bases, naval stations, and depots in the Eastern and Southern Theater Commands is estimated to hold 5–10 million barrels of JP-8 and naval diesel, sufficient for one to two weeks of high-tempo operations without drawing on national reserves. Strategic ashore reserves include at least five major underground sites at Huangdao, Jinzhou, Zhanjiang, and Huizhou, with underground capacity approaching 130 million barrels. The PLA Navy's afloat replenishment fleet — nine Type 903/903A Fuchi-class and three Type 901 Fuyu-class ships — provides approximately 1 million-plus barrels of mobile fuel reserves.[8]

The drawdown model distinguishes three degradation scenarios: a 20% daily shortfall is absorbable for 6–12 months; a 30% shortfall leads to collapse in 2–4 months; a 50% shortfall (~112,500 bpd deficit) likely becomes unsustainable within 2–3 months, not because reserves run dry but because delivery infrastructure fails under attrition.

APP-G
Initial PLA Fuel System Sensitivity Analysis
U.S. maritime interdiction is the single most powerful lever: 75% import denial causes fuel exhaustion by Day 155 in the high-scenario, compared to Day 562 with no interdiction — a ~400-day difference.
U.S. refinery strikes are the second most decisive action: 30% refinery degradation accelerates exhaustion by ~228 days; 50% degradation causes collapse within 3 months. On the Chinese side, the three most important internal levers are: (1) civilian fuel redirection — increasing from 15% to 30% extends PLA endurance by approximately 6 months; (2) refinery output continuity — protecting refineries extends PLA sustainment by over three years; and (3) strategic reserve size — doubling reserves from 600 million to 1.2 billion barrels provides only a 3–4 month buffer. The key interaction finding is that maritime interdiction and refinery strikes compound each other. Port disruption alone does not cause collapse — it requires combination with interdiction or refinery attacks to become decisive.

APP-H
Sustaining 900,000 bpd JP-8 Fuel Delivery in a Taiwan Conflict
Heritage assessed that the United States and allies are unlikely to sustain the full 900,000 bpd JP-8 aviation fuel requirement across a 365-day Taiwan conflict without degradation. The sustainable estimate under optimal conditions is 600,000–700,000 bpd.
Under wartime conditions involving PLA interdiction, contractor withdrawal, or political friction, throughput may fall to 500,000–600,000 bpd. The resultant shortfall of 200,000–400,000 bpd would necessitate strict sortie prioritization, drawdown of prepositioned reserves, and potential grounding of lower-tier missions. Route A (Andersen AFB and Kadena AB) is assessed as capable of 300,000–400,000 bpd initially but is "highly vulnerable to missile strikes, pipeline interdiction, and port chokepoint disruption." The overall conclusion: "fuel logistics — not aircraft losses — would likely be the gating constraint on U.S. and allied airpower tempo in a high-intensity Taiwan conflict."[9]

§02 HORMUZ CRISIS // 2026-03-31
§02

The 2026 Iran Conflict & Strait of Hormuz Crisis

Scale of the Disruption

On February 28, 2026, joint U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran — including the killing of Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei — triggered an Iranian decision to close the Strait of Hormuz, the waterway through which approximately 20% of the world's oil and significant LNG volumes transit daily. The Strait was formally closed on March 4, 2026. Tanker transits collapsed from over 150 vessels per day to a daily average of approximately 13 — a 95% reduction. The IEA declared the Iran conflict was causing the "largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market," with global supply expected to plunge by 8 million bpd in March 2026 alone.[12][13][14][1]

Brent crude surged past $100 per barrel on March 8 — the first time in four years — and reached a peak of approximately $126 per barrel before partially retreating. As of late March, Brent was trading around $88.9/barrel and WTI at approximately $84.9/barrel, following the IEA's announcement of the largest-ever coordinated strategic reserve release of 400 million barrels. Gulf producers (Kuwait, Iraq, Saudi Arabia, and UAE) collectively reduced output by a reported 6.7 million bpd by March 10 and at least 10 million bpd by March 12. QatarEnergy declared force majeure on all LNG exports; on March 18, Iranian strikes hit Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex, causing a 17% reduction in Qatar's LNG production capacity that analysts estimate will take 3–5 years to repair.[15][1][12]

China as the Pivotal Energy Actor

China, the world's largest crude oil importer at approximately 11.1 million bpd, is simultaneously the country most exposed to the Hormuz closure and the most effectively insulated from its immediate effects. Approximately 40–50% of China's crude oil imports transit the Strait of Hormuz. Yet China entered the crisis with an estimated 1.39–1.5 billion barrels in strategic and commercial oil reserves — roughly 120–150 days of net imports at 2025 consumption levels — plus approximately 46 million barrels of Iranian crude held in floating storage off Dalian and Zhoushan.[16][17][18]

In January–February 2026, China imported 96.93 million tons of crude oil — a 16% increase from the same period the prior year — interpreted as deliberate pre-positioning ahead of anticipated Middle East escalation. Russia simultaneously set a record, shipping 1.92 million bpd to China in February 2026, providing a Hormuz-independent supply stream. Saudi Arabia and the UAE retain pipeline capacity to reroute a combined 5 million bpd via the Yanbu terminal, bypassing the strait. As a result, despite holding more at-risk Hormuz-dependent imports than India, South Korea, or Japan, China's Shanghai Index fell only 0.7% in the immediate aftermath of the conflict onset, compared to South Korea (-10.2%), Japan (-6.5%), and Taiwan (-3.8%).[17][19][18]

Iran's "Friendly Nations" Corridor

Iran's Foreign Minister announced that "friendly nations" — specifically China, India, and Russia — are permitted to navigate through a designated "safe corridor" between Larak and Kish islands in Iranian territorial waters. The first Chinese oil tanker transited the Strait of Hormuz on March 23, 2026. This arrangement formalizes a geopolitical alignment: Iran is using access to its most strategically critical waterway to reward nations it perceives as ideological or economic partners, and to punish those aligned with the United States and Israel.[20][14]

⚠

Strategic Significance: The "friendly nations" corridor arrangement means that any future U.S. leverage over China through chokepoint pressure must now account for Iran's willingness to selectively exempt Beijing from energy disruptions — a dynamic absent from TIDALWAVE's original baseline models.

§03 TW VS REALITY // 2026-03-31
§03

How the Iran Conflict Changes the TIDALWAVE Analysis

1

China's Strategic Reserves: The Baseline Was Too Conservative

TIDALWAVE's most consequential baseline error concerns the scale of China's strategic petroleum reserves. Appendix A estimated China's SPR at approximately 684 million barrels; Appendix G modeled the high scenario at 1.2 billion barrels. The actual figure, as confirmed by multiple sources during the crisis, appears to be approximately 1.4–1.5 billion barrels of combined strategic and commercial reserves. This is roughly 3.6 times the size of the U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve.[21][18][3]

In TIDALWAVE's Scenario A sensitivity analysis, a 1.2 billion barrel reserve extended PLA endurance to Day 562 under 75% import interdiction. A 1.4–1.5 billion barrel reserve would extend this further, likely pushing endurance past Day 600 under equivalent interdiction pressure. Critically, China also added approximately 400 million barrels to its reserves during 2025 alone — and if the current conflict forces China to draw on reserves rather than continue adding to them, it directly contradicts the five-year plan announced to expand strategic reserves, as analysts at the Irregular Warfare Center note. The implication: every barrel China burns to compensate for Hormuz-diverted supply is a barrel not available for a hypothetical Taiwan conflict's fuel endurance calculation.[22]

2

Hormuz is Closed by Iran, Not Blockaded by the U.S. Navy

TIDALWAVE's Appendix G explicitly identified the Strait of Malacca as the primary U.S. chokepoint leverage point against China, assessing with high confidence that a blockade of Malacca "would very likely cut off China's crude oil supply line at its source." The assumption was that the U.S. Navy would be the interdicting force. In the current crisis, the Strait of Hormuz — not Malacca — is the disrupted chokepoint, and Iran is the actor closing it, not the United States.[23]

This inversion changes the strategic calculus in several critical ways. China receives preferential corridor access through Hormuz as a "friendly nation," partially insulating it from the disruption. The Strait of Malacca is now absorbing diverted oil flows that would previously have transited Hormuz, increasing its throughput and highlighting the shift in energy trade geography. If the U.S. were to simultaneously pressure the Malacca Strait — either in the context of a Taiwan conflict or as escalatory leverage over China — it would face the double-bind of already having degraded Hormuz traffic through the Iran operation.[24][20][17]

3

U.S. Military Attention and Capacity Is Bifurcated

TIDALWAVE's Appendix C modeled U.S. force deployment on the assumption that the U.S. military's full logistical capacity would be oriented toward a Taiwan conflict. The current reality is that the United States is actively conducting military operations to reopen the Strait of Hormuz — a campaign begun on March 19, 2026. Major shipping firms including Maersk, CMA CGM, and Hapag-Lloyd have suspended operations through the strait, creating pressure on U.S. Navy escort capacity. The fleet oilers and sealift assets identified in Appendices B, D, and I as the critical bottleneck for a Taiwan operation are the same assets being consumed by Middle East operational demands.[12][17][5]

Defense News reported on March 19, 2026 that China has actually reduced PLA warplane sorties toward Taiwan since the Iran conflict began, with daily sortie counts falling from typically a dozen or more to as few as two or three on several days. Analysts attribute this to a political calculation: China likely does not want to provoke the United States while Trump is simultaneously managing the Iran war and scheduling a summit with Xi Jinping in late April. This suggests that the Iran conflict, paradoxically, has temporarily reduced the near-term Taiwan threat risk — but at the cost of consuming the very logistics assets TIDALWAVE identifies as already insufficient for sustained Indo-Pacific combat.[25]

4

The JP-8 Supply Chain: New Pressure on Existing Shortfalls

Appendix H found that the U.S. and allies could not sustain 900,000 bpd JP-8 across a 365-day Taiwan conflict, with a projected shortfall of 200,000–400,000 bpd. The Iran conflict adds two new pressures to this picture. First, jet fuel prices have surged more than 80% since the conflict began, increasing the cost of every sortie — including the gray-zone operations both sides conduct daily near Taiwan — and straining allied defense budgets. Second, the JP-8 supply chain depends on Middle Eastern refining output and tanker logistics networks that are now severely disrupted. Refinery capacity at risk exceeds 4 million bpd globally due to the Hormuz closure. If Gulf refineries — which process significant quantities of jet-grade kerosene exports — remain constrained into Q2 2026, the upstream JP-8 production base assumed in TIDALWAVE's logistics models becomes less reliable.[13][9][25]

5

Allied Cooperation Under Fiscal and Energy Stress

TIDALWAVE's Route B assessment in Appendix H projected that Japan, Australia, the Philippines, Singapore, and India could collectively deliver 200,000–300,000 bpd of JP-8, but noted "political volatility and limited infrastructure threaten its long-term reliability." The Iran crisis amplifies all of these risks. Japan's stock markets fell 6.5% in response to the Hormuz closure; South Korea — a key logistics node for U.S. forces — fell 10.2%. Every allied economy dependent on Middle Eastern energy imports is now managing its own supply crisis, fiscal strain, and political pressure to prioritize domestic energy security over U.S. basing support agreements. This materially increases the likelihood of the "political reversals" and "host-nation reversals" flagged in Appendix H as capable of reducing throughput by 30,000–70,000 bpd.[18][9]

6

Taiwan's Own Energy Crisis

Perhaps the most underappreciated dimension of the current disruption is its effect on Taiwan itself. Taiwan relies on imports for 95% of its energy needs, including over 99% of its oil and natural gas, and approximately 70% of its crude oil comes from the Middle East. Taiwan holds approximately 150 days of oil in reserve but only 11 days of natural gas inventory — far below South Korea and Japan. Qatar, which accounts for roughly a third of Taiwan's LNG imports, has declared force majeure and suspended exports; the Ras Laffan LNG complex damage may take 3–5 years to repair.[26][1]

TIDALWAVE modeled Taiwan as the geographic objective of a Chinese invasion scenario. The current energy crisis reveals a parallel vulnerability: a prolonged Hormuz disruption could weaken Taiwan's economic and political resilience even without a shot being fired across the Taiwan Strait, constraining its ability to fund defense procurement and sustain public confidence ahead of any Chinese coercive pressure campaign. CSIS wargame analysis, cited in parallel ASPI research, found that natural gas runs out in Taiwan in approximately 10 days of a blockade — a timeline the current crisis has now rendered acutely visible.[27]

§05 STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS // 2026-03-31
§05

Strategic Implications

China Is a Net Beneficiary of the Iran Crisis — For Now

China entered the Hormuz crisis better positioned than any other major oil importer: larger reserves, preferential corridor access, a record Russian pipeline supply, and the political capital of being publicly neutral while privately pressuring Iran to keep Chinese tankers moving. The crisis has, in the short term, demonstrated the resilience of China's multi-year energy security investment strategy and validated the warning in TIDALWAVE's executive summary that Beijing was deliberately stockpiling toward conflict readiness.[28][29][20][22][21]

However, the crisis is not costless. China's $89 billion in Middle Eastern infrastructure investments (2019–2024) — including stakes in Qatar's North Field LNG expansion — have been damaged by Iranian strikes. Chinese goods shipments through the Strait of Hormuz have been paralyzed alongside oil flows, affecting manufacturing supply chains. And most critically, every barrel of oil China burns now to compensate for Hormuz disruption is unavailable for the Taiwan conflict scenario modeled in TIDALWAVE.[29]

The TIDALWAVE Vulnerability Thesis Becomes More Urgent, Not Less

The report's core conclusion — that U.S. fuel logistics, not combat power, is the binding constraint in an Indo-Pacific conflict — is reinforced by current events. The Iran conflict has demonstrated in real-time that a single maritime chokepoint closure can paralyze 20% of global oil supply within days. If the United States is managing a Middle East military campaign while preparing for an Indo-Pacific contingency, the fleet oiler and sealift capacity identified in Appendices B, D, and I as structurally insufficient is now even more strained. Heritage's own follow-up report, "Build the Golden Fleet" (March 24, 2026), reiterates the finding that the Combat Logistics Force is the primary throttle on sustained naval operations — a vulnerability that has not been addressed in the months since TIDALWAVE's publication.[30][11]

The Malacca Chokepoint Becomes More, Not Less, Critical

The Hormuz disruption has re-routed energy flows toward the Strait of Malacca, which is now carrying a higher proportion of global energy trade. Nearly 80% of China's oil imports, including all Malacca-transitable supply, must pass through this chokepoint. TIDALWAVE identified Malacca interdiction as the single most powerful strategic lever against China's fuel endurance — assessed with high confidence that closure "would very likely cut off China's crude oil supply line at its source." As Hormuz becomes a contested, disrupted corridor, Malacca's strategic importance increases further. Any U.S. strategic planner considering leverage over China must now account for the fact that China has demonstrated it can weather a Hormuz disruption for months; the Malacca leverage point is the one China has not yet been forced to test.[23][24]

Key Analytical Finding: As Hormuz-dependent flows divert through Malacca, the strait's strategic importance to any future Taiwan contingency escalates in direct proportion to the duration of the current Hormuz crisis.
