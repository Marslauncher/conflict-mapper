Origins & Initiation

On February 28, 2026, US and Israeli forces launched a coordinated joint strike against Iran in what represented the largest Western military operation in the Middle East since the 2003 Iraq invasion. The operation's most consequential act was the targeted killing of Iranian Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, along with senior IRGC commanders, during strikes on the Pasteur Street leadership compound in Tehran. Israeli forces dropped over 1,200 bombs in the first 24 hours of the campaign, targeting nuclear facilities, missile production sites, air defense infrastructure, and leadership nodes simultaneously. US B-2 Spirit stealth bombers delivered GBU-57 Massive Ordnance Penetrators (MOPs) against hardened underground facilities at Natanz, Fordow, and Isfahan.

The strategic objective was to permanently degrade Iran's nuclear weapons program, missile production infrastructure, and the IRGC command structure that directs Iran's regional proxy network. Years of diplomatic failure and Iran's accelerating uranium enrichment program — reaching 440kg of 60%-enriched uranium sufficient for approximately 10 nuclear weapons — provided the immediate trigger. The killing of Khamenei represented a deliberate decapitation strike intended to cause leadership paralysis.

Escalation Dynamics

Iran's retaliation was swift and massive. Between March 1-5, the IRGC launched over 500 ballistic missiles and approximately 2,000 Shahed drones targeting US military bases across Qatar, Kuwait, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Bahrain, and Jordan. The strikes damaged THAAD radar systems, destroyed US tactical operations centers, damaged KC-135 tanker aircraft, and killed at least 12 US personnel in multiple incidents, including six killed when two KC-135s collided over Iraq on March 12. Iran has maintained continuous missile and drone attacks — with IRGC reporting over 80 waves of strikes by late March.

Hezbollah entered the conflict on March 2 with rocket attacks on Israel, prompting IDF ground operations into southern Lebanon beginning March 16. By March 24, Israel announced plans to control all territory south of the Litani River. The Houthi movement in Yemen officially joined the war on March 28, firing missiles at Israel's Eilat. The conflict has cascaded from a bilateral US-Israel vs. Iran confrontation into a regional war drawing in Iran's entire "axis of resistance."

📊 CURRENT STATE // DAY 29 ASSESSMENT

US Operation "Epic Fury" is ongoing with continuous air strikes and naval operations. The Pentagon has requested $200 billion from Congress and is planning "weeks-long limited raids" for potential ground operations, with 3,500 Marines aboard USS Tripoli positioned for potential deployment.

IDF Operation "Roaring Lion" is reportedly "days away" from completing its strike package against Iran's military infrastructure. CENTCOM claims destruction of 2/3 of Iran's arms manufacturing capacity, though US intelligence assesses only 1/3 of Iran's missile arsenal has been confirmed destroyed, suggesting significant survivability of underground and dispersed systems.

Diplomatic Track: Backchannel negotiations are ongoing via intermediaries. An April 6 deadline set by President Trump for energy infrastructure strikes has created a narrow window for a possible ceasefire or temporary halt. Iran is publicly demanding international condemnation and sanctions relief while privately signaling willingness to discuss terms through Omani intermediaries.

Post-Decapitation Leadership

IRGC as de facto power
📌 Leadership Situation — Day 29

The killing of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei on Day 1 has fundamentally destabilized Iran's political structure. The velayat-e faqih (Guardianship of the Islamic Jurist) system is built around a single supreme authority figure. His son Mojtaba's appointment lacks religious legitimacy — he is not a Grand Ayatollah — making the IRGC, which controls the security apparatus, the effective governing power. The regime has not collapsed but is operating under unprecedented stress.

Uncertain Status

Why No Popular Uprising?

Despite the catastrophic damage to Iran's military infrastructure, economy, and leadership, mass popular protests against the regime have not materialized — for several key reasons:

Factor 1
IRGC January Crackdown
In January 2026, during pre-war tensions, the IRGC conducted a preemptive crackdown on opposition networks, protest organizers, and civil society leaders. Thousands were killed and tens of thousands arrested, effectively decapitating protest infrastructure before the war began.
Factor 2
Wartime Nationalism
Foreign military strikes on Iranian soil have historically generated rally-around-the-flag nationalism even among Iranians who oppose the regime. The killing of Khamenei, while welcomed by some, has been framed domestically as a foreign assassination of national sovereignty.
Factor 3
Internet Blackout
Iran has implemented near-total internet blackouts since Day 2, preventing coordination, suppressing dissent, and controlling information flow. VPN usage has been criminalized with shoot-on-sight orders for those attempting to breach the digital blockade.
Factor 4
Surveillance State
Iran's expansive domestic surveillance apparatus — facial recognition, phone tracking, informant networks — makes open protest essentially impossible. The IRGC Basij has been fully mobilized for domestic control operations.

The Stimson Center's analysis identifies this outcome as an "IRGC Republic" — a scenario in which the Revolutionary Guard transitions from a parallel power structure to the outright governing authority, using the wartime emergency to permanently subordinate clerical institutions. In this model, the new Supreme Leader becomes a ceremonial figurehead while the IRGC Coordination Council makes all strategic and domestic security decisions. Historical precedent from post-war Iraq and post-sanctions Russia suggests this kind of institutional consolidation can persist for decades even under sustained international pressure.

The fragmentation pathway most closely resembles the Syria and Libya models: proxy groups that previously operated under centralized IRGC command begin acting autonomously as payroll and command-and-control chains dissolve. Hezbollah, Houthi leadership, and Iraqi Shia militias each have their own institutional interests that may diverge sharply from Tehran's directives. Simultaneously, competing IRGC factions — the Quds Force remnants, the Basij domestic wing, and regional corps commanders — may pursue separate agendas, with provincial power centers in Isfahan, Khuzestan, and Khorasan emerging as competing nodes of authority rather than a unified resistance.

A counterintuitive dynamic observed in multiple conflict studies suggests that external military pressure may paradoxically consolidate the Iranian regime rather than weaken it. The rally-around-the-flag effect, amplified by information blackouts and wartime nationalism, has historically strengthened authoritarian governments under foreign attack — including Iran's own experience during the Iran-Iraq War, when Khomeini leveraged Saddam's invasion to suppress internal opposition and cement revolutionary institutions. The same logic may apply here: absent a decisive military collapse or internal mutiny, the IRGC has strong incentives to frame negotiation as betrayal rather than pragmatism.

09 // GROUND OPERATIONS

Ground Assault Planning

Pentagon: limited raids, not full invasion
📋 Pentagon Planning Assessment

The Pentagon is planning for potential "weeks-long limited ground raids" against specific Iranian coastal and island targets. This is NOT a full-scale ground invasion of Iran — which would require 500,000+ troops. The objective is targeted seizure operations against Kharg Island oil infrastructure and IRGC naval bases to establish leverage for negotiations and degrade Iran's ability to fund further military operations.

Available Ground Forces

Current Positioning
3,500 Marines — USS Tripoli
A Marine Expeditionary Unit (MEU) aboard the USS Tripoli (LHA-7) amphibious assault ship is positioned and ready for ground operations. Includes MV-22 Ospreys, AH-1Z Vipers, and ACE aviation combat element.
STATUS: Positioned for deployment orders
▼
Option A
Kharg Island Seizure
Amphibious assault on Kharg Island to seize oil terminal infrastructure and cut Iran's remaining oil export revenue. The island is ~1/3 the size of Manhattan with ~90% of Iran's crude exports. Seizure would cut the regime's financial oxygen. ~2,500-3,500 Marines required.
OBJECTIVE: Deny Iran oil revenue, create negotiating leverage
▼
Option B
IRGC Naval Base Raids
Helicopter-borne raids against IRGC naval bases at Bandar Abbas, Qeshm, and Larak Island to destroy remaining fast attack craft, shore-based anti-ship missiles, and mine warfare infrastructure. Would secure Hormuz transit for tankers.
OBJECTIVE: Open Hormuz, destroy naval threat capability

Obstacles & Geography

Zagros & Alborz Mountains
Iran's interior is protected by two major mountain ranges — the Zagros (rising to 4,500m) along the western and southwestern border, and the Alborz along the north. Any land invasion from Iraq or the Gulf coast faces immediate mountain chokepoints. Historical parallel: the Shah fell partly because of these terrain features.
2,500 km Coastline
Iran's coastline along the Persian Gulf and Gulf of Oman provides extraordinary defensive depth for the IRGC Navy. Multiple hardened naval bases, shore-based missile batteries, and a vast mine warfare capability make amphibious operations costly. IRGC has trained for exactly this scenario for 40 years.
Historical Precedent: Tanker War 1987-88
During Operation Earnest Will (1987-88), the US escorted Kuwaiti tankers through the Gulf under Iranian threat. The Iranian Ajr minelayer was captured. This operational template — limited naval operations without ground invasion — is the model for current planning. It worked then; whether it works against a more capable 2026 IRGC is uncertain.

IRGC Naval Control Strategy

The IRGC Navy's Hormuz strategy is based on creating a "Sea of Chaos" — not necessarily physically blocking the strait with a mine field (which would invite overwhelming US response) but imposing de facto control through constant harassment, threat of attack, and requiring vessels to obtain IRGC clearance for passage. This approach:

  • Drives up insurance costs for tankers (up 400%+)
  • Forces buyers to pay IRGC-aligned firms for clearance certificates
  • Generates intelligence on which countries are buying Iranian oil
  • Maintains deniability — traffic hasn't "stopped," it's just controlled
  • Threatens to escalate to full closure at any moment
⚠ Current Hormuz Status

Iran is demanding control over the strait and has established a de facto checkpoint system. Traffic is limited to vessels with special IRGC clearance. This is effectively a partial blockade — not a full closure but with severe economic impact equivalent to 30-40% reduction in oil flow.

12 // STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS

Impact on Taiwan Scenario

US force and stockpile diversion
🌏 China Watching Closely

Chinese military analysts and PLA observers are drawing systematic lessons from the US-Iran conflict about US capabilities, vulnerabilities, and the feasibility of US intervention in a Taiwan scenario. The conflict is simultaneously draining US resources and providing PLA with invaluable operational intelligence.

📦
Munitions Stockpile Depletion
The conflict has consumed hundreds of JASSM-ER, thousands of SM-3 interceptors, hundreds of Tomahawks, and 20+ GBU-57 MOPs. US production rates for precision munitions cannot quickly replace expenditures. AEI analysts warn that continued Iran operations will "hollow out" Pacific war reserves needed for Taiwan contingency — exactly as the Iran-Iraq war analysis predicted in the Wargame briefings.
⚓
Naval Assets Committed
Multiple carrier strike groups, Aegis destroyers, and submarine forces are committed to Middle East operations. The 5th Fleet and repositioned 6th Fleet assets are unavailable for Pacific contingency. China benefits from this reduced deterrence posture in the short term — US would struggle to fight two simultaneous major wars.
🎓
PLA Lessons Learned
PLA analysts are studying: (1) How Iran's ballistic missiles penetrated THAAD defenses — applicable to Taiwan Strait scenarios. (2) How base vulnerability forced US operational changes — relevant to Guam and Japan basing. (3) How the US diplomatic position was constrained by ally concerns — China may exploit similar dynamics around Taiwan.
🕐
Opportunity Window
Military analysts debate whether the US Middle East commitment creates a genuine "window of opportunity" for China regarding Taiwan. Most assess that US nuclear deterrence and remaining Pacific forces are still sufficient to deter China. However, the window is real for a limited coercive operation — a naval blockade rather than full invasion.
📉
Deterrence Signal
The Iran strikes simultaneously demonstrate US military capability and willingness to use force (positive deterrence signal for Taiwan) while revealing vulnerabilities and resource constraints (negative signal). Chinese decision-makers must weigh both factors. The most sophisticated analysis suggests the strikes may slightly strengthen deterrence by demonstrating US resolve.
💬
Diplomatic Bandwidth
The Iran conflict consumes enormous US diplomatic bandwidth — senior officials, congressional attention, allied coordination. This reduces bandwidth for Taiwan deterrence diplomacy, Indo-Pacific institution building, and the complex signaling required to maintain strategic ambiguity. China benefits from US distraction even without military action.

Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Kuwait are benefiting from elevated oil prices — projecting $50-100B in additional revenues. However, the war creates profound security risks for Gulf monarchies that outweigh financial benefits. They are balancing between US alliance obligations and domestic stability.

Current Trajectory

IDF "Days Away" from Completing Strike Package

The IDF has indicated it is close to completing its planned strike package against Iran's military infrastructure. This creates a natural inflection point: Continue to escalate toward energy infrastructure? Or shift to negotiations?

April 6 Deadline: Trump's public deadline for the energy infrastructure decision creates artificial urgency. Either: (1) Iran concedes enough to pause operations, or (2) Kharg Island and Iran's energy sector are targeted, causing massive global economic shock.

Diplomatic Tracks Active

Oman is serving as the primary intermediary between Tehran and Washington. Qatar is facilitating indirect talks between Hamas-adjacent figures and regional actors. The back-channel negotiations are reportedly more advanced than public statements suggest, with Iran privately signaling openness to a nuclear freeze in exchange for ceasefire and sanctions discussions.

Can This Be "Won"?

  • Temporary degradation of Iran's nuclear program (2-5 year delay)
  • Destruction of 2/3 of missile/drone production capacity (CENTCOM claims achieved)

  • Forcing Iran to negotiate under pressure
  • Creating leverage for nuclear deal with stronger verification
