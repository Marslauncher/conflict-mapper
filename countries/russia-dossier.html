S-01

GEOGRAPHIC OVERVIEW

Russia spans 11 time zones across Eurasia, possessing extraordinary strategic depth, vast Arctic frontage, and resource wealth that shapes its geopolitical calculus.

STRATEGIC TERRAIN

Russia's landmass — 17.1 million km² — provides unparalleled strategic depth exploited against Napoleonic and Nazi invasions alike. The North European Plain is the traditional invasion corridor; the Urals serve as an industrial redoubt. Siberia holds ~80% of Russia's hydrocarbon reserves and vast mineral deposits that underpin the defense-industrial base.

ARCTIC FRONTIER

Russia claims 1.2 million km² of additional Arctic continental shelf including the Lomonosov Ridge. The Northern Sea Route — increasingly navigable due to climate change — offers a strategic shipping lane shorter than the Suez Canal. Russia has dramatically expanded Arctic basing, reopening Soviet-era facilities at Tiksi, Naryan-Mar, and Wrangel Island.

High Priority
BORDER COMPLEXITY

Russia shares borders with 14 nations and maritime boundaries with the US and Japan. The 2014 annexation of Crimea and 2022 invasion have reshaped the strategic border with Ukraine. NATO's eastern flank now extends 1,300+ km closer to Russian territory following Finland's 2023 accession — a historic reversal of Russian buffer-state strategy.

RESOURCE GEOGRAPHY

Russia holds the world's largest natural gas reserves, second-largest coal reserves, and eighth-largest oil reserves. Its vast resource endowment funds defense spending but also creates structural dependencies on commodity prices. Sanctions since 2022 have disrupted export routes, accelerating a strategic pivot to Asian energy markets.

S-02

LEADERSHIP & POLITICAL DOCTRINE

Vladimir Putin, President since 2000 (with a 2008–2012 interval as PM), operates a personalist authoritarian system where strategic decisions concentrate in a small inner circle.

CLICK TO ENLARGE
VLADIMIR PUTIN
PRESIDENT // RF
Decision Authority — Absolute

Putin is a former KGB officer who rose through the St. Petersburg political machine under Anatoly Sobchak before being appointed FSB Director in 1998 and Prime Minister in 1999. His strategic worldview combines KGB-era statecraft, Orthodox nationalist ideology, and a conviction that Western liberal hegemony is an existential threat to Russian civilization. He has articulated this in extended essays, including the 2021 "On the Historical Unity of Russians and Ukrainians" which provided ideological scaffolding for the 2022 invasion. Carnegie

The Security Council — chaired by Putin — is the effective decision-making body for national security. Nikolai Patrushev (former Secretary, now Presidential Advisor), Sergei Shoigu (former Defense Minister, now Security Council Secretary), and Valery Gerasimov (Chief of the General Staff) represent the key institutional nodes. Defense Minister Andrei Belousov, appointed 2024, signals an integration of economic and military planning unusual in Russian governance.

S-03

NUCLEAR DOCTRINE

On November 18, 2024, Putin signed an executive order updating Russia's nuclear deterrence fundamentals — lowering the threshold for first use and expanding triggering conditions.

// 2020 DOCTRINE (SUPERSEDED)

EXISTENTIAL THRESHOLD

Nuclear weapons could be used in response to conventional attack only if "the very existence of the state is threatened." High threshold, implied last resort.

  • Nuclear or WMD attack on Russia
  • Conventional attack threatening state existence
  • No sovereignty/territorial provisions
// NOV 19, 2024 UPDATE — ACTIVE

CRITICAL THREAT THRESHOLD

Nuclear response authorized for "aggression with conventional weapons creating a critical threat to Russia's sovereignty and/or territorial integrity." Significantly lower bar. ELN Carnegie

  • Conventional attack threatening sovereignty OR territory
  • Non-nuclear state attack with nuclear power "support" = joint attack
  • Launch-on-warning authorized for large-scale aerospace strikes
  • Attack on Russia by NATO member = aggression by full alliance
  • WMD use against Russian forces anywhere, including overseas
ANALYST ASSESSMENT
Carnegie Endowment / Stimson Center / European Leadership Network
Coercive Intent Assessed

The November 2024 update was timed precisely with the Biden administration's authorization of Ukrainian strikes into Russian territory using US-supplied ATACMS. Carnegie's Nicole Grajewski assessed the doctrine as "primarily coercive manipulation" rather than a genuine operational blueprint — Moscow's nuclear decisions "would likely not involve Putin carefully parsing doctrinal clauses." The Stimson Center concluded the US should maintain measured deterrence rather than radical strategy shifts in response. Stimson Carnegie

ELECTRONIC WARFARE CAPABILITY

Russia fields the world's most sophisticated tactical EW ecosystem, including Krasukha-4 (anti-satellite/radar jamming), Murmansk-BN (strategic HF jamming range 5,000+ km), Moskva-1 (passive radar), and Richag-AV helicopter-borne EW pods. Ukraine theater has become a live test of Russian EW vs. Western countermeasures — results have been mixed, with Ukrainian adaptability consistently surprising Russian planners. Gerasimov has cited EW integration as a core lesson of the conflict.

S-06

FOREIGN POLICY DOCTRINE

Russia's strategic doctrine integrates Eurasianist ideology, near-abroad dominance, Arctic expansionism, and non-Western multilateral institutions — SCO and CSTO — to construct a counter-hegemonic bloc.

EURASIANISM

Drawing on Aleksandr Dugin's Neo-Eurasian philosophy and earlier Eurasianist thinkers (Trubetzkoy, Savitsky), Putin's Russia rejects Western liberal universalism and asserts a distinct Eurasian civilizational identity. The ideology posits Russia as the natural center of a Eurasian heartland stretching from Lisbon to Vladivostok — antithetical to the "Atlanticist" (US-dominated) order. This provides the ideological foundation for anti-NATO posturing, the SCO, and the Eurasian Economic Union.

NEAR ABROAD DOCTRINE

Russia asserts a sphere of privileged interests across the 14 post-Soviet states — the "near abroad." This manifests as: frozen conflicts maintained as coercive levers (Transnistria, South Ossetia, Abkhazia, Nagorno-Karabakh until 2023); economic integration pressure via the Eurasian Economic Union; and military presence through CSTO. The 2022 invasion of Ukraine represents the most extreme expression of this doctrine, triggered by NATO's eastward expansion and Kyiv's Euro-Atlantic pivot. Eurasia Center

ARCTIC CLAIMS

Russia has submitted extended continental shelf claims to the UN Commission on the Limits of the Continental Shelf covering the Lomonosov Ridge, the Mendeleyev Ridge, and the Alpha Ridge — totaling ~1.2 million km² of additional Arctic seabed. These areas are believed to hold ~30% of the world's undiscovered natural gas and 13% of undiscovered oil. Russia has militarized the Arctic extensively: Arctic Command established 2014, six Arctic military bases reopened, Trefoil fortress operational (Franz Josef Land).

SCO & CSTO

Putin views the Shanghai Cooperation Organization as "one of the key pillars of a fair, multipolar world order" and advocates building a "new architecture of Eurasian security" through SCO-CSTO-CIS integration — explicitly replacing "Europe-centric and Euro-Atlantic models." At the 2024 Astana summit, 92% of Russia's SCO trade was conducted in non-Western currencies. The CSTO — Russia's military alliance — lacks Chinese membership and serves as Moscow's primary military lever in Central Asia. Hudson Institute
