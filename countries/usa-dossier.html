The U.S. Army maintains unmatched conventional superiority with advanced combined-arms capability. ⚡ HYPERSONIC WEAPONS RACE — STATUS 2026

The U.S. has accelerated hypersonic weapon programs after initially falling behind China and Russia. The AGM-183A ARRW reached full-rate production decision in 2025, providing B-52H bombers a Mach 20+ strike capability. The Army's LRHW "Dark Eagle" batteries are deploying to the Pacific, directly countering China's DF-series ballistic missiles and filling the range gap created by the INF Treaty's collapse. The Navy's Conventional Prompt Strike (CPS) system for Virginia and Zumwalt-class hulls has an IOC of ~2028. These systems collectively close the hypersonic gap with China's arsenal within the next 24–36 months.

07 // Nuclear Posture & Triad

The United States maintains a nuclear arsenal of approximately 5,177 total warheads, with ~1,700 deployed on the strategic nuclear triad. A $946 billion modernization program over 2025–2034 is replacing all three legs of the triad simultaneously — a historically unprecedented undertaking.

☢ NUCLEAR FORCE OVERVIEW — MARCH 2026

Total arsenal: ~5,177 warheads (FAS estimate, 2025). Deployed strategic: ~1,419. Deployed non-strategic: ~100 B61-3/4 bombs in Europe (NATO Nuclear Sharing). Reserve/awaiting dismantlement: ~3,618. New NPR guidance (2025 Trump NPR revision) emphasizes flexible response options and the resumption of low-yield nuclear weapons testing consideration. The 2025 NPR explicitly names China as a near-peer nuclear competitor, establishing a 2-peer planning baseline requiring simultaneous deterrence of Russia and China — a first in US nuclear history.

NUCLEAR TRIAD — ALL THREE LEGS

LEG 1 — ICBM (GROUND)
Minuteman III / Sentinel
450 Minuteman III ICBMs in hardened silos across Wyoming, North Dakota, and Montana. Each armed with up to 3 W78 or W87 reentry vehicles (MIRV capable). 30-min flight time to Russia. Being replaced by LGM-35A Sentinel (~2029 IOC), a $125 billion program providing increased accuracy, reliability, and survivability.

Key stats: 450 deployed silos | Range: 13,000+ km | W78 (335kt) or W87 (300kt) warheads | CEP ~90m
LEG 2 — SLBM (SEA)
Ohio-class SSBN + Trident II D5
14 Ohio-class SSBNs provide the most survivable leg. 8-10 boats at sea on alert at any given time. Each carries 24 Trident II D5 missiles with up to 8 W76-1 or W88 warheads (MIRV). Post-START ceilings limit to 24 missiles per boat. Columbia-class (12 planned) will replace Ohio beginning ~2031.

Key stats: 14 SSBNs | 24 missiles each | Range: 7,400+ km | W76-1 (100kt) or W88 (475kt) | CEP <90m
LEG 3 — NUCLEAR BOMBER
B-52H + B-2A + B-21 Raider
76x B-52H (with B61-12, B83, ALCM, JASSM-ER) + 19x B-2A Spirit (penetrating strike) + B-21 Raider entering service. The B61-12 tail-kit converts legacy gravity bombs into precision nuclear weapons. B-21 replaces B-2 and B-1B fleet with vastly improved survivability against modern IADS.

Key stats: 76 B-52H + 19 B-2A + B-21 (100+ planned) | B61-12 (50kt dial-a-yield) | ALCM (200kt, 2,500km)

08 // Special Operations Forces

U.S. Special Operations Command (USSOCOM) headquartered at MacDill AFB, Florida oversees approximately 70,000 Special Operations Forces deployed across 70+ countries simultaneously. SOF provides the U.S. an unmatched capability for direct action, counterterrorism, unconventional warfare, and influence operations.

ARMY — JSOC TIER 1
Delta Force (1st SFOD-D)
The U.S. Army's most elite Tier 1 direct action and counter-terrorism unit, operating under JSOC. Conducts hostage rescue, HVT capture/kill, and sensitive site exploitation globally. Classified manning (~1,000 operators). Selection via SFAS + 6-month OTC. Operators deploy under JSOC alongside SEAL Team Six (DEVGRU). Operates AC-130s, MH-60M Black Hawks, and classified platforms via SOAR support.
NAVY — JSOC TIER 1
DEVGRU / SEAL Team Six
Naval Special Warfare Development Group — the Navy's Tier 1 counter-terrorism and special reconnaissance unit. Conducted Operation Neptune Spear (OBL raid, 2011). Operates in 4 assault squadrons, with specialized maritime, assault, and support elements. Selection from NSW community. Estimated ~300–400 assault operators. Primary seaborne, airborne, and urban counter-terrorism mission set.
ARMY TIER 2
75th Ranger Regiment
The Army's premier direct action light infantry force, comprising 3 active battalions (~3,600 Rangers). Conducts airfield seizure, raid operations, and surge direct action in permissive and contested environments. The "Ranger Ready Force" maintains 18-hour global response. Not Tier 1, but provides JSOC with scalable assault capability and is the most tactically proficient conventional-caliber force in existence.
AIR FORCE TIER 1
24th Special Operations Wing
Air Force SOF including combat controllers (CCT), special reconnaissance (SR), and AFSOC AC-130 gunship crews. Combat Controllers embed with Army SF and SEAL teams providing air traffic control and close air support in denied areas. The 160th SOAR (Night Stalkers) provides dedicated aviation support to all JSOC forces with MH-60M, MH-47G, and AH/MH-6M platforms.
ARMY UW / FID
Special Forces (Green Berets)
1st through 20th Special Forces Groups — the backbone of US unconventional warfare (UW) and foreign internal defense (FID). Organized into 12-man ODAs with regional language and cultural expertise. 5 active groups (1st, 3rd, 5th, 7th, 10th SFG) aligned to all geographic commands. Conducted large-scale UW in Ukraine advisory mission.
NAVY SOF
SEAL Teams 1–10
Sea-Air-Land teams provide direct action, special reconnaissance, and counter-terrorism across maritime, riverine, and urban environments. 8 SEAL Teams plus DEVGRU and SEAL Delivery Vehicle Teams. ~2,700 operators in the Naval Special Warfare community. Extensively deployed to the Red Sea (Counter-Houthi operations), Philippines, and Baltic region 2025–2026.

10 // Military Doctrine

U.S. military doctrine evolved from Cold War NATO defense through post-9/11 counterterrorism to today's Multi-Domain Operations (MDO) and Joint All-Domain Command and Control (JADC2) framework designed for great power competition. The 2026 NDS emphasis on "integrated deterrence" shapes all operational concepts.

JOINT ALL-DOMAIN COMMAND AND CONTROL
JADC2 — The Overarching Operational Concept

JADC2 is the Pentagon's overarching command and control concept linking sensors and shooters across all domains — land, sea, air, space, and cyberspace — into a single, AI-assisted decision network. The goal: any sensor feeding any shooter in milliseconds, regardless of service or domain. JADC2 represents a fundamental shift from service-centric stovepipes to a fully networked "kill web."

Service implementations: Army's Project Convergence (ground sensor/effector integration), Air Force's Advanced Battle Management System (ABMS), Navy's Project Overmatch (maritime kill web). These feed into the classified Combined Joint All-Domain Command and Control (CJADC2) layer for multinational operations.

Status (2026): Partial operational capability across INDOPACOM. Full JADC2 fielding is estimated 2028–2030. The Pentagon's Defense Innovation Unit (DIU) and DARPA are driving accelerated AI/ML integration. Starlink plays a key tactical communications backbone role per Ukraine lessons.

MULTI-DOMAIN OPERATIONS
Army FM 3-0 — Competing, Penetrating, Dis-Integration, Exploitation

Multi-Domain Operations (MDO), as articulated in Army Field Manual 3-0 (2022), describes how U.S. forces will compete with near-peer adversaries across all domains simultaneously. MDO posits that future conflict will not be sequential (air supremacy → ground advance) but will require simultaneous operations in land, sea, air, space, and cyber domains to converge effects and overwhelm adversary decision cycles.

The four MDO tenets: Compete (persistent presence/posture short of armed conflict), Penetrate (breach adversary Anti-Access/Area Denial [A2/AD] systems), Dis-integrate (break apart integrated defensive systems), Exploit (decisive action in created windows). This framework is explicitly designed to counter China's A2/AD strategy in the Indo-Pacific.

Pacific application: Long-Range Fires Battalions (HIMARS/PrSM) positioned on Philippine islands. INDOPACOM's "Archipelago Defense" places Army precision fires inside the second island chain to deny China maritime maneuver space.

NUCLEAR POSTURE REVIEW 2025
2-Peer Deterrence + Integrated Deterrence

The 2025 Nuclear Posture Review (classified; summary declassified March 2026) establishes the first two-peer nuclear deterrence baseline in US history, explicitly requiring simultaneous deterrence of Russia and China — each of which will field near-peer nuclear arsenals by the early 2030s.

Key policy shifts: Retains "no first use" ambiguity (does not adopt formal NFU). New emphasis on flexible response options including low-yield nuclear weapons (W76-2 SLBM warhead, B61-12 dial-a-yield). Extended deterrence assurances to Japan, South Korea, and Australian partners reinforced. NATO nuclear sharing commitment maintained (B61-12 deployed to Aviano, Lakenheath, and 3 other European bases).

Concerns: The Congressional Budget Office projects $946 billion in nuclear modernization costs over the next decade, straining conventional procurement budgets. STRATCOM commander publicly noted in 2024 that current nuclear force posture was designed for a one-peer world.

SPECIAL OPERATIONS DOCTRINE
SOF-Centric Operations & Persistent Engagement

Twenty-plus years of GWOT forged a SOF-centric operational model centered on persistent F3EAD targeting cycles (Find, Fix, Finish, Exploit, Analyze, Disseminate). The JSOC Tier 1 direct action model — married to national intelligence (NSA/NGA feeds) and airpower (AC-130J, MQ-9) — became the US military's most reliable offensive tool.

Current pivot: USSOCOM is transitioning from GWOT-era CT focus toward great power competition support: building partner nation capacity (FID/COIN), conducting Information Operations, and competing in the gray zone against Russian/Chinese influence operations.

Ukraine precedent: SOF-embedded advisory mission (classified, ~350+ total) provided Ukraine with targeting support, HIMARS training, Stinger/Javelin integration, and intelligence feeds that fundamentally altered the battlefield — all short of declared hostilities. This "advisory warfare" model is now doctrine.

NAVAL DOCTRINE — DISTRIBUTED MARITIME OPERATIONS
DMO — From Concentrated to Distributed Lethality

Distributed Maritime Operations (DMO) emerged from the recognition that concentrating 11 carriers in traditional CSG formations creates predictable, targetable formations vulnerable to China's DF-21D/DF-26 "carrier killer" anti-ship ballistic missiles. DMO disperses naval lethality across many smaller, harder-to-target platforms — including DDGs operating independently with long-range missiles, LCSs, unmanned surface vessels, and submarine forces.

Key concept: "Distributed Lethality." Every surface combatant becomes a potential strike platform. DDG-51 Flight III ships receive SM-6 anti-ship capability. The Constellation-class FFG adds another ASW/anti-surface layer. USVN 38 (South Pacific) is experimenting with USV/UUV distributed strike formations.

Red Sea validation (2024–2026): Op Epic Fury tested CSG doctrine against saturation drone/missile attacks. Two CSGs expended ~$1.2B in SM-2/SM-6/SM-3 interceptors, prompting urgent review of sustainable intercept economics vs. adversary cheap drone saturation.

11 // Foreign Policy & Alliances

Under the second Trump Administration, U.S. foreign policy has undergone a significant reorientation — from liberal internationalism toward transactional bilateralism, burdensharing demands on allies, and explicit "America First" prioritization of Western Hemisphere objectives.

⚑ TRUMP ADMINISTRATION FOREIGN POLICY — KEY PIVOTS (2025–2026)

The Trump Administration's foreign policy features several sharp departures from prior doctrine: NATO allies pressured to spend ≥5% GDP on defense (vs. 2% threshold), with Article 5 guarantees made explicitly conditional on compliance. Ukraine ceasefire negotiations led directly by U.S. as mediator (not party), with Zelenskyy invited to White House March 2025 — talks ongoing but fragile. Greenland acquisition rhetoric elevated, claiming national security imperative. Canada sovereignty rhetoric (51st state) creating bilateral friction. Panama Canal reassertion language in NDS. Simultaneously, Gulf state ties strengthened (Saudi Arabia mega-arms deal, UAE partnerships). Tariff-driven trade war with China continuing.
